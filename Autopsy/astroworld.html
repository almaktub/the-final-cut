Overall Score
"Travis made an album about a demolished amusement park and somehow made it the most immersive record of his career. The concept does all the heavy lifting the lyrics don't need to."
9.1 because the production architecture on this record is its own argument. Mike Dean, Cactus Jack, Tame Impala, James Blake, Pharrell - Travis assembled collaborators who all operate at maximum, and the album holds them without feeling like a compilation. CAROUSEL is Frank Ocean at his most playful over a sample that has no business being this good. STOP TRYING TO BE GOD arrives like a seance: Stevie Wonder's harmonica, Kid Cudi's hums, James Blake's voice building to a closer that feels genuinely earned. The 0.9: after NC-17 the album runs out of momentum. ASTROTHUNDER is gorgeous but brief. YOSEMITE is filler. WHO? WHAT! exists mainly as a gap in the sequencing. The back four tracks needed either cutting or restructuring. COFFEE BEAN lands the album back on its feet but the damage is done. A 9.1 album that could have been a 9.6 with a better edit.

Memory as Architecture
Six Flags AstroWorld was demolished in 2005 when Travis was 14. The album is not about the park - it is the park. Every production choice is a reconstruction of what it felt like to be a kid inside something larger than yourself. The concept works because Travis doesn't explain it. He just builds it.
🌆
Houston as Subject
R.I.P. SCREW is the most direct - a tribute to DJ Screw, the Houston producer who invented the slowed-and-chopped sound that defines the city's musical identity. But Houston is in the album's DNA whether Travis names it or not. The warmth, the sprawl, the sense of physical scale. It's a city record that never announces itself as one.
🎤
The Feature as Instrument
STOP TRYING TO BE GOD has Stevie Wonder, Kid Cudi, and James Blake. CAROUSEL has Frank Ocean. WAKE UP has The Weeknd. Travis treats his features the way a director treats cast - each one chosen for what they bring to the specific moment, not for cross-promotion. Frank Ocean on a Donkey Kong Country sample is a creative decision. Most of these are.
🎛️
Production over Lyricism
Travis is not the strongest lyricist in his class. On Astroworld, he doesn't need to be. The production is the argument, and the argument is airtight. He understood what kind of record he was capable of making and made exactly that record. Self-awareness is underrated in rap.

Track by Track
The Dissection
01 STARGAZING
9.0 ▼
The cold open. Disorienting, layered, a beat flip halfway through that resets the tone entirely. Travis announcing the park is open without saying so. The distorted vocals are the wristband going on.
02 CAROUSEL (feat. Frank Ocean)
9.8 ▼
The best track. Frank Ocean doing the loosest, most joyful thing he's done in years over a sample nobody else would have touched. The production lets him breathe and he takes full advantage. Proof that Travis's best skill is knowing who to put in the room.
03 SICKO MODE (feat. Drake)
9.4 ▼
Three beats, three moods, one song that held the number one spot for three weeks. The transitions shouldn't work and work completely. Drake's verse is one of his better performances in this era. The production does most of the heavy lifting but the heavy lifting gets done.
04 R.I.P. SCREW (feat. Swae Lee)
8.8 ▼
A direct tribute to DJ Screw - slowed, hazy, Houston-drenched. Swae Lee's hook is the right kind of effortless. Not the album's most ambitious track but one of its most honest.
05 STOP TRYING TO BE GOD (feat. James Blake, Kid Cudi, Stevie Wonder)
9.6 ▼
Seven minutes. Stevie Wonder's harmonica arrives two-thirds in and changes the room. Kid Cudi hums underneath everything. James Blake closes it. The sequencing within the track is better than the sequencing on most albums. The most architecturally ambitious thing here.
06 NO BYSTANDERS (feat. Juice WRLD, Sheck Wes)
8.9 ▼
The mosh pit track. Sheck Wes yelling over a beat that sounds like a carnival ride going too fast. Juice WRLD's verse is effortless. This is the album at its most physical.
07 SKELETONS (feat. Pharrell, Tame Impala, Gunna)
8.7 ▼
Psychedelic, Tame Impala's production making the album briefly float. Pharrell does what Pharrell does. A left-field detour that works better than it should on paper but doesn't fully justify its placement between NO BYSTANDERS and WAKE UP.
08 WAKE UP (feat. The Weeknd)
9.1 ▼
The Weeknd sounds like himself in the best way. The sample underneath the track gives it a warmth the back half needs. One of the album's most replayable moments.
09 5% TINT
8.6 ▼
Travis alone, a beat that rolls rather than hits. A palette cleanser that knows it's a palette cleanser. Does what it needs to do and doesn't try to do more.
10 NC-17 (feat. 21 Savage)
9.2 ▼
21 Savage arrives and the album briefly becomes a different record - harder, colder, more stripped. His verse is one of the better guest turns here. The production underneath is some of the most interesting on the album.
11 ASTROTHUNDER
8.8 ▼
Beautiful and too short. The album catching its breath after NC-17. The synths here are doing something genuinely cinematic. At two minutes it feels like a sketch of something that should have been developed further.
12 YOSEMITE (feat. NAV, Gunna)
8.3 ▼
Where the album's momentum starts leaking. NAV and Gunna both do their thing but their thing isn't what this moment needs. The beat is fine. The track is the album's low point not because it's bad but because of what surrounds it.
13 CAN'T SAY
8.5 ▼
Travis finding a pocket and staying in it. Nothing extraordinary but nothing wasted. A track that would sit comfortably in the middle of the album and is slightly out of place near the end.
14 WHO? WHAT!
7.9 ▼
The album's weakest point. Pharrell and Quavo both appear but the track never finds a reason for them to be there. It exists between ASTROTHUNDER and COFFEE BEAN and earns nothing from the company it keeps.
15 COFFEE BEAN
9.3 ▼
The right way to end. Travis alone, a piano, something genuinely vulnerable beneath the bravado. The album landing softly after everything it put through the speakers. If the closer were a 7 this would be an 8.8 album. It isn't.

Footnote
Astroworld was named after Six Flags AstroWorld, a Houston theme park that closed in 2005 when Travis Scott was 14 years old. The park sat empty for two years before being demolished. Travis has said it was a formative place for him growing up, and the album was his attempt to recreate the feeling of being inside it. The annual Astroworld Festival began in 2018 the same year the album dropped. The cover art by Cactus Jack features Travis's face as an entrance gate.
