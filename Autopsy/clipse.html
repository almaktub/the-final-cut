Album Autopsy · 055
Clipse
Let God Sort Em Out
Independent · 2025 · Hip-Hop
Sixteen years in the wilderness and they came back with Pharrell, Kendrick, Nas, and a Grammy - the most anticipated rap reunion of the decade delivered everything it owed
0.0
The great rap reunion album. Pusha T and No Malice returned after sixteen years with the same cold precision, the same moral weight, and the same Pharrell - and somehow it sounded like no time had passed at all.
The premise was already loaded: Clipse, whose last album dropped in 2009, reuniting with Pharrell Williams - the architect of their entire sonic world - on a self-released independent project. No major label, no compromise, no hype machine needed. The album debuted at No. 4 on the Billboard 200 and earned five Grammy nominations including Album of the Year. The production is Pharrell in full command - not nostalgic for The Neptunes era but evolved past it, the beats warmer and more orchestral without losing the precision that made their classic records feel like lock mechanisms clicking into place. Pusha is sharper than he's been in years. Malice - No Malice for most of the last decade - returns from his faith journey with bars that carry the weight of a man who has actually reckoned with what he used to do. The guest list is star-studded and every feature earns its place. This is not a legacy cash-in. It is a legitimate statement from a group that had something left to say.
Recurring Themes
⚖️
Judgment as Reckoning
The title announces the album's central theology: God sorts, not man. But the album is more conflicted than that phrase suggests - it is full of men who have done things that require sorting, who are not sure which pile they belong in. Pusha raps about the drug trade with the same unflinching specificity he always has, but the frame has changed. The reckoning is no longer hypothetical.
🕊️
Faith and Its Complications
Malice's arc is the album's moral spine. He left rap for years, found faith, and came back. Let God Sort Em Out doesn't ask him to pretend the faith away or pretend the past away - it holds both. The tension between what he believed then and what he believes now is the most interesting thing on the record, and the album is smart enough not to resolve it cleanly.
💎
Luxury as Language
Clipse have always used luxury goods as a specific kind of code - not braggadocio but inventory, the language of a certain economy. LGSEO continues this tradition while adding age and perspective. The material references now carry a different weight: these are men who made it out, who know what the cost was, and who haven't decided whether it was worth it.
🔄
Return and Its Meaning
An album about coming back - from absence, from the street, from silence, from estrangement. Every track is implicitly in dialogue with the 16-year gap. The reunion is not just biographical; it's structural. The album asks what it means to return to something after you've become a different person, and answers, imperfectly and honestly: you bring both selves with you.
"Let God sort em out - I been waiting on the verdict / Been guilty all my life but I swear the time was worth it."
Let God Sort Em Out / Chandeliers (feat. Nas)
Easter Eggs & Annotations
The Sessions
Two Years at Louis Vuitton Paris
Pharrell - now Men's Creative Director at Louis Vuitton - invited Pusha and Malice to Paris to craft the album over two years of sessions. The context is not incidental: Clipse's aesthetic has always been about the criminal-luxury overlap, the drug money that buys the same things as generational wealth. Recording in Paris at a fashion house while rapping about the same streets they always have is exactly the tension the album is built on.
The Independence
Self-Released, No. 4 on Billboard 200
LGSEO was released independently - no major label, no distribution deal of record. It debuted at No. 4 on the Billboard 200, Clipse's highest chart position since their 2002 debut Lord Willin'. The industry has spent 20 years consolidating leverage over artists, and Pusha walked back in the door with a Grammy-nominated independent project. The business statement is as loud as the music.
The Collaboration
Chains & Whips: Kendrick Lamar
Kendrick Lamar's appearance on "Chains & Whips" - which earned a Grammy nomination for Best Rap Performance - is the album's most discussed guest spot. Three of the most technically precise rappers of their respective eras on one Pharrell beat. Pusha is cold and clinical. Malice is philosophical. Kendrick arrives and the temperature drops further. They just rapped. No ceremony required.
The Name
No Malice Returns as Malice
No Malice - who had publicly distanced himself from rap, from the drug-trade subject matter, and from the Clipse identity for much of the prior decade - came back as Malice on this record. The name change back is not explained in interviews. It is explained in the music: a man who has made peace, imperfectly, with all his selves at once. His verses carry a moral seriousness that Pusha's bars gain from by proximity.
Track-by-Track
Chains & Whips (feat. Kendrick Lamar)
9.9
The album's centerpiece and its most Grammy-discussed track. Three of the most technically precise rappers of their respective eras on a Pharrell beat that gives each of them exactly the space they need. The Grammy nomination for Best Rap Performance was not a courtesy - this is one of the best rap performances in recent memory, and the three-way dynamic is impossible to imagine anyone else pulling off.
The Birds Don't Sing (feat. John Legend & Voices of Fire)
9.7
Nominated for Best Rap Song and the album's most sonically ambitious track - a gospel-inflected Pharrell production featuring John Legend and the Voices of Fire choir. The contrast between the choir's transcendence and Clipse's unflinching lyricism is the album's central tension made explicit. It shouldn't work as cleanly as it does. It works completely.
Let God Sort Em Out / Chandeliers (feat. Nas)
9.7
The title track and the album's thesis statement. Nas brings a veteran's weight that matches Clipse's - three men who have been in the game long enough to know what sorting means and to have some doubts about where they land. The two-part structure mirrors the album's overall movement: from street to something else, from certainty to reckoning.
P.O.V. (feat. Tyler, the Creator)
9.5
Tyler's feature is the album's most unexpected and most earned. Where Kendrick met Clipse on their terrain, Tyler shifts the perspective - literally about point of view, the way the same events look from different positions in the chain. Pharrell's production is the most playful on the record, and Tyler thrives in the space. Clipse grounds him; he loosens them.
E.B.I.T.D.A. (feat. Pharrell Williams)
9.4
The album's most explicit engagement with the business-as-crime metaphor - EBITDA is an accounting term, the vocabulary of the boardroom applied to the street. Pharrell raps, which he does rarely and well. The track makes the case that the drug economy and the corporate economy operate on the same logic, and does it over a beat that makes you feel the argument before you understand it.
By the Grace of God (feat. Pharrell Williams)
9.3
The closer and the album's most explicitly devotional track. Malice is most fully himself here - the faith isn't performative, it's the genuine article, arrived at through the specific experience of a decade of distance and return. Pharrell's production is stripped to just space and the voices in it. A quiet, serious way to end a loud, serious album.

Sixteen years is a long time to wait for an album. Long enough for Pusha T to build a solo catalog, for No Malice to become a preacher, for Pharrell to go from Virginia Beach to Paris, for the entire rap landscape to shift twice over. The question with any reunion this delayed is whether the wait was worth it - whether there was something left to say, or whether nostalgia was doing the work that music should be doing. Let God Sort Em Out answers the question decisively. There was something left. The Grammy confirmed it. The music had already.
