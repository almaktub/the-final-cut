Miles Davis
Kind of Blue
Columbia Records · 1959

Five tracks. Two sessions. Mostly first takes. The musicians had never seen the charts before recording. The most influential jazz album ever made - and the most listenable. Those two things are almost never the same thing.
0.0
Overall Score
"The argument against Kind of Blue being a 10.0 is that it's 'accessible.' As if accessibility were a character flaw."
10.0 because it did something no album before or since has managed: it changed the fundamental grammar of music in real time, on first takes, with musicians who had never played the songs before, and it sounds better today than anything else recorded in the same decade. Modal jazz - using scales instead of chord progressions as the basis for improvisation - gave the musicians room to breathe, and what they breathed was perfect. Coltrane, Cannonball Adderley, Bill Evans, Paul Chambers, Jimmy Cobb - all playing at the height of their powers on material they'd only just seen. Miles barely spoke between takes. That was the instruction: play the space.

The Modal Revolution
Bebop required navigating rapid chord changes - technically demanding, emotionally constricting. Miles wanted more space. Modal improvisation gave musicians a scale to explore rather than a harmonic minefield to cross. The album is built on that freedom. Every instrument has more room than it's used to, and every musician knows what to do with it.
⬜
Negative Space
Miles Davis understood silence the way painters understand negative space - as a structural element, not an absence. The pauses on Kind of Blue are compositional. What he doesn't play defines the shape of what he does. Bill Evans described this as "the sense of the continuous." Every note implies what surrounds it.
🎙️
The First-Take Genius
Miles handed the musicians rough sketches - scales, moods, structures - moments before recording. So What, Flamenco Sketches, Blue in Green: heard for the first time in the studio, recorded with minimal preparation. The freshness isn't incidental. It was the method. Miles wanted the musicians discovering the music in real time. The listener hears them find it.
📀
The Accessible Avant-Garde
Kind of Blue is the best-selling jazz album in history. It sold to people who'd never bought a jazz record in their lives, and to musicians who changed entire careers after hearing it. Usually an album does one of those things. The fact that it does both - and does both without compromising anything - is the argument for 10.0.

Track by Track
The Dissection
01 So What
9.9 ▼
The bass figure that opens the album is one of the most recognizable phrases in jazz - a question-and-answer between Paul Chambers and Bill Evans that establishes the mode before Miles arrives. When his trumpet enters it has already been waited for. "So What" is the sound of someone arriving with exactly what the room needed. The title is the attitude: cool, unhurried, assured. Everything that follows builds from that entrance.
02 Freddie Freeloader
9.7 ▼
The only track featuring Wynton Kelly on piano instead of Bill Evans - and the most blues-rooted moment on the album. Named after a real figure from Miles's circle. Cannonball Adderley's alto solo here is among the most joyful things committed to a jazz record: warm, conversational, impossible to resist. The rare track on Kind of Blue that smiles.
03 Blue in Green
9.8 ▼
The most debated track on the album - Miles and Evans both claimed to have written it, and the truth is probably that they wrote it together. A ten-bar circular form that creates a sense of hovering, of suspended time. Miles's muted trumpet here is so interior it sounds like a thought rather than a sound. Coltrane's solo is barely present and absolutely necessary.
04 All Blues
9.7 ▼
A 6/4 waltz built on a 12-bar blues structure - familiar enough to feel rooted, strange enough to feel new. The horn players trade solos over the walking bass and the result is something that sounds simultaneously ancient and invented. Jimmy Cobb's ride cymbal is one of the most elegant pieces of jazz drumming ever recorded: not propulsive, not decorative, just present.
05 Flamenco Sketches
9.9 ▼
The album's most open structure: five scales, each held as long as the soloist chooses before moving on. No fixed length, no predetermined shape - only the scale and the decision of when to leave it. Miles moves through the five modes like rooms in a house he knows intimately. Coltrane's solo here is pure patience. The album ends not with resolution but with open space, which was always the point.

Footnote
The original pressing of Kind of Blue was recorded at the wrong speed - a piano was flat by a small margin, and for decades listeners heard a slightly de-tuned album without knowing it. The 2001 remaster corrected the pitch. Both versions are still in circulation. The wrong one sounds fine. That's how good the playing was.
