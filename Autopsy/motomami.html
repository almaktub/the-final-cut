Overall Score
"Flamenco, reggaeton, trap, and bossa nova in a blender - and it holds together because she is the constant, not the genre."
9.3 because MOTOMAMI does what almost no pop album manages: it sounds genuinely new without sounding like it's trying to. The collage of influences isn't fusion in the smooth sense - it's more like Rosalía picked up whatever was in arm's reach and used it, and her taste is sharp enough that the results cohere. SAOKO and BULERÍAS are the twin poles around which the album orbits - one a pure velocity statement, one a return to where she started. The fact that both belong to the same record is the whole argument. The 0.7: some of the shorter interstitial tracks feel more like mood between the peaks than complete ideas in themselves.

What This Album Is Really About

🔴
Self as Constant
The album changes genre every few tracks and stays completely coherent because Rosalía's voice and presence are the organizing principle. MOTOMAMI is a word she invented to describe a specific duality - softness and hardness, the moto and the mami - that is hers alone.
💃
Tradition as Raw Material
Flamenco doesn't disappear on this album - it relocates. The palmas, the percussive breath, the emotional architecture of deep song appear in unexpected containers. BULERÍAS makes this explicit. The rest of the album lives the argument instead of making it.
⚡
Power and Vulnerability
The MOTOMAMI compound contains a contradiction: speed and softness, aggression and tenderness. The album moves between these modes without apology or explanation. The title names the tension and declines to resolve it.
🏍️
The Body in Motion
Motorcycles, dance, physical transformation - the album is full of bodies moving through space. Where El Mal Querer was still and ceremonial, MOTOMAMI is kinetic. The music wants to move you first and explain itself later.

Track by Track
The Dissection
01SAOKO
9.6▼
The album's statement of purpose in two minutes. The Destiny's Child sample chopped into pure rhythm, Rosalía on top declaring transformation without sentimentality. 'Yo me transformo' and then the beat drops and she proves it immediately. The best opening track she's made.
02CANDY
8.8▼
A sharp left turn from the opener. Delicate where SAOKO was percussive, sweet where it was hard. The contrast is deliberate: the album refuses to let you predict its next move from the previous one.
03BULERÍAS
9.4▼
The flamenco heart of the record, uncut. Just voice and palmas and the ancient rhythmic form of bulerías. Everything else on MOTOMAMI earns credibility because this exists on the same album. She's not playing with tradition. She is tradition, choosing to play.
04LA FAMA
9.0▼
The Weeknd feature works because neither of them tries to cross into the other's territory. Bossa nova rhythm under a song about fame as a destructive lover. The metaphor is obvious; the execution makes it feel earned.
05PÁJAROS AMARGOS
8.7▼
A brief classical vocal interlude that provides genuine space in the album's compressed architecture. Bitter birds. The title is exact. Thirty seconds of something that sounds like it's from a different century, sitting comfortably between trap and flamenco.
06MOTOMAMI
9.1▼
The title track is smaller and stranger than you'd expect from an album with this much momentum behind it. A dembow rhythm, a playful delivery, and a lyric that explains the word she invented. It works by being unpretentious when it could have been a monument.
07HENTAI
9.2▼
Starts as a delicate piano ballad and refuses to become what you expect. A song about desire that takes the subject as seriously as desire deserves. The transition from soft to hard in the final stretch is one of the album's best produced moments.
08G3 N15
8.5▼
An interstitial track where the production concept - the title is leet for 'GENESIS' - is more interesting than the song itself. But in context it functions as breath and passage. Not every track needs to be a statement.
09BIZCOCHITO
9.3▼
The most purely fun track on the record and one of the most precisely executed. A reggaeton beat treated with classical care - the arrangement of something this intentionally lightweight is harder than it sounds. Every accent is placed right.
10DIABLO
8.9▼
A sharp track about a specific kind of menace - the charming kind. The production leans into the devil reference without becoming theatrical about it. One of the tougher tracks on an album that's mostly interested in different kinds of softness.
11SAKURA
8.6▼
A Japanese pop influence absorbed into the album's texture without being decorative. The cherry blossom as shorthand for beautiful impermanence lands here because the vocal performance earns the image rather than just wearing it.
12CHICKEN TERIYAKI
9.0▼
Absurd title, exact execution. The hardest track on the record production-wise - a drill-adjacent beat that would seem dissonant anywhere else and makes perfect sense here. Rosalía goes fully aggressive on the delivery and the contrast with the songs around it is the point.
13DELIRIO DE GRANDEZA
8.7▼
Delusions of grandeur sung about with the precision of someone who has examined her own ego and found it interesting rather than shameful. The bachata influence is handled with exactly the care she brings to flamenco.
14LA COMBI VERSACE
8.8▼
A late-album gem. The Versace tracksuit as emblem of a specific kind of Spanish working-class aspiration. Rosalía wearing luxury and street together without irony. The track knows what it's doing.
15CUÍDATE
8.9▼
Take care of yourself. A closing instruction that doesn't sentimentalize. The album ends here in mood even if not in track order - a dispersal rather than a finale, which is exactly right for music that never stayed still long enough to be pinned down.

Footnote
Rosalía worked on MOTOMAMI for three years, reportedly writing and discarding hundreds of demos. She handled most of the production herself alongside a small group of collaborators. The album was recorded during her relationship with Rauw Alejandro, whom she later became engaged to before they separated. The personal material is in the album, distributed quietly across the record, which is exactly how she chose to carry it.
