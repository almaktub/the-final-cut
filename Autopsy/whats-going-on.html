Motown's smoothest voice. America's hardest questions. The most urgent 35 minutes in soul music history - and Marvin had to threaten retirement to get it released.
0.0
Overall Score
"Nine tracks. Thirty-five minutes. No filler. Every song a movement in the same argument. It doesn't ask for your attention - it assumes it."
10.0 because there is nothing here to subtract. Not a song to cut, not a production choice to second-guess, not a lyric that doesn't earn its place. The album works as a suite - What's Going On bleeds into What's Happening Brother bleeds into Inner City Blues as one continuous statement about a country at war with itself and its own people. Marvin recorded it while Motown's parent label was resisting its release, while his brother came home broken from Vietnam, while Detroit still bore the scars of the '67 riots. The pressure found the exact right shape. This is what 10.0 means: an artist at the height of their powers, at the exact moment in history where those powers were necessary, making something the world wasn't ready for and needed anyway.

🪖
The Soldier Returned
The album is narrated by a returned Vietnam veteran. Marvin's brother Frankie served three tours. The opening question - "What's going on?" - is the soldier looking at America after the war and not recognizing what he fought for. Everything that follows is the answer he finds.
🌍
Black Ecology
Mercy Mercy Me connects race to environment a decade before it was a mainstream conversation. The oil wasted on the ocean, the fish full of mercury, the air we can't breathe - Marvin understood that environmental destruction lands hardest on the people with the fewest exits. That's still true.
🙏
The Spiritual Beneath the Political
God Is Love and Wholy Holy are not interludes. They're the album's structural argument: that the political crisis is inseparable from a spiritual one. Marvin wasn't making a protest record. He was making a prayer that happened to be indistinguishable from a protest record.
🎙️
Motown vs. Marvin
Berry Gordy called it "the worst thing I ever heard" and refused to release it. Marvin said he would never record for Motown again if it wasn't put out. Gordy relented. It became Marvin's best-selling album and changed the direction of soul music. The most important record label dispute in pop history ended with a 10.0 album.

Track by Track
The Dissection
01 What's Going On
9.9 ▼
The most political question ever disguised as casual conversation. Marvin layered three vocal tracks over each other - one formal, one loose, one almost whispering - so that the song sounds like a community talking at once. The most famous party chatter in pop music opens a protest record. That was the move.
02 What's Happening Brother
9.4 ▼
The soldier's reentry. Searching for work, trying to get his bearings, asking if there's still a place for him. Written with Marvin's brother Frankie in mind. The confusion in the lyric is not poetic - it's documentary.
03 Flyin' High (In the Friendly Sky)
9.5 ▼
Drug addiction as the logical response to the world the album has described. The "friendly sky" is heroin. The production is so lush and warm that the horror sneaks up on you. Marvin understood that suffering looks comfortable from the outside.
04 Save the Children
9.6 ▼
The album's most direct political statement - and it still comes wrapped in strings and layered harmonies so warm you almost don't notice the urgency. "Who really cares / to save a world in despair?" is not rhetorical. Marvin was asking himself.
05 God Is Love
9.3 ▼
The briefest track and the theological pivot. If the previous songs are the diagnosis, this is the proposed cure - which the rest of the album is honest enough to admit is not sufficient. A ninety-second gospel declaration that holds the album's contradictions in one place.
06 Mercy Mercy Me (The Ecology)
9.9 ▼
Environmental justice as soul music. "Oil wasted on the ocean and upon our seas / fish full of mercury" was 1971. The fact that it's more relevant now than when Marvin recorded it is the most damning evidence for the album's thesis. The arrangement builds from piano to full orchestral drift. Every instrument arrives like bad news.
07 Right On
9.2 ▼
The album's most jazz-inflected moment - loose, almost conversational in structure, with a flute lead that keeps slipping into unexpected registers. The longest track on the record and the one that most clearly shows where Marvin's head was: a Black musician claiming jazz's intellectual tradition for soul music.
08 Wholy Holy
9.5 ▼
A hymn that doesn't believe God has answered. Marvin's faith wasn't uncomplicated - and this track holds both the devotion and the doubt. "We shall overcome" appears not as a slogan but as a genuine question about whether it's still possible to believe that.
09 Inner City Blues (Make Me Wanna Holler)
9.8 ▼
The album ends not with hope but with honesty. Inflation, crime, the draft, poverty - the litany of what the soldier came home to. The bass groove is one of the darkest Motown ever produced. Marvin doesn't resolve anything. He ends the album mid-crisis, which is the only honest thing to do. The song fades back into the opening of What's Going On, making the whole thing a loop. There is no exit.

Footnote
The album ends with Inner City Blues fading into the opening bars of What's Going On - creating an infinite loop. Marvin structured it so there is no resolution, no exit from the conditions the album describes. The loop was intentional. It still is.
