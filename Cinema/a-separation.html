Final Cut No. 8

A Separation

dir. Asghar Farhadi  ·  2011  ·  Drama  ·  123 min
10.0
Out of ten
A courtroom drama where nobody is lying and nobody is innocent and you cannot locate a villain anywhere.

Everyone in this film is right. Nader is right to stay for his father. Simin is right to want a better life for her daughter. Razieh is right to need the job even if she couldn't do it without compromising her religion. Hodjat is right to be furious about what happened to his wife. And every single one of them lies, a little, at the moments when the truth would cost too much. Farhadi builds the film like a machine designed to produce sympathy equally in all directions, and it works so precisely that audiences in every country report siding with different characters and all of them being correct.

The film's central event — did Nader know Razieh was pregnant when he pushed her? — is genuinely unknowable because Farhadi never shows us what Nader knew. This is not a cheat. It's the point. The legal system must produce a verdict from ambiguity, which is what legal systems always do and rarely admit. Razieh's oath on the Quran is the film's hinge: she swears to tell the truth on a text she believes in absolutely, and then must decide in real time whether the truth she tells will destroy her family. Her hesitation takes about four seconds. It's the most devastating four seconds in the movie.

Farhadi shoots in handheld close-up, always at conversation distance, never pulling back for relief. The camera walks out of the courtroom with the two parents at the end, leaving Termeh inside to tell a judge which parent to live with. We don't hear her answer. The film ends before she speaks, which is the only answer available: we've been watching two people's lives for two hours and we still don't know which choice is right. Neither does she. Neither would you.

"What is wrong is wrong, no matter who says it."
Nader — A Separation
Rewatch verdict
Rewatch It
The second watch reveals exactly how carefully Farhadi withholds — every scene is constructed to give you the thing next to the truth.
