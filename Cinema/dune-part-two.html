Dune: Part Two

dir. Denis Villeneuve  ·  2024  ·  Sci-Fi / Epic  ·  166 min
9.0
Out of ten
The second half of the best science fiction film made in decades.

Villeneuve completes what Part One began, and the second half of Herbert's novel is the harder material. The messianic arc turns dark, the hero's journey inverts, the chosen one trope gets interrogated rather than celebrated. Paul Atreides becomes something the film is not entirely comfortable with, and Villeneuve is honest about that discomfort even when the spectacle demands a different register. Chalamet plays the turn with a controlled coldness that's more unsettling than overt villainy would be. The film doesn't fully commit to that darkness in its ending — that is its main compromise, and it's worth naming — but it goes further than most Hollywood productions would dare with a character they've spent two films making you root for.

Zendaya has the space here that Part One denied her, and she uses it. Her Chani is the film's sceptic, the one character who looks at the prophecy and refuses to be consumed by it, and her arc ends where it should. Austin Butler as Feyd-Rautha is a physical performance — all stillness and contained menace — that earns every scene he's in. The sandworm riding sequence is the single best use of IMAX since, probably, Part One. Greig Fraser's cinematography makes Arrakis feel genuinely alien in a way that forty years of Dune adaptations never managed.

What Villeneuve has made across these two films is a science fiction epic that takes its source material seriously as a political text, not just as world-building. Herbert wrote a book about the danger of charismatic leaders and the willingness of populations to surrender judgment to them. The film holds onto that argument even when the franchise logic pulls against it. This is ambitious filmmaking that mostly lands, and in the current landscape, mostly is more than enough.

"Long live the fighters."
Paul Atreides — Dune: Part Two
