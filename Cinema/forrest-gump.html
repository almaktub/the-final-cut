Final Cut No. 53

Forrest Gump

dir. Robert Zemeckis  ·  1994  ·  Drama / Comedy  ·  142 min
8.5
Out of ten
A film so earnest about sentimentality that it almost turns sentimentality into something respectable.

The film has aged into controversy in ways its 1994 audience didn't anticipate. The conservatism of its historical reading, Forrest succeeds while the counterculture fails, is a real ideological position dressed as innocent observation. Every radical or countercultural figure in the film is punished, diminished, or killed. Forrest, who asks no questions and disturbs no systems, is rewarded. Whether that makes the film dishonest or just politically specific is the argument worth having. What's not arguable is Tom Hanks' performance: he plays a man of limited self-awareness with complete self-consistency, never condescending to the character, never winking at the audience about the irony of his situation. It's one of the technical achievements of 1990s American acting.

The Vietnam sequences are the film's most honest, not because they're antiwar exactly, but because they don't aestheticize the danger the way Hollywood usually does. Forrest doesn't understand what's happening and the film doesn't explain it to him, which means the chaos lands with unusual weight. Robin Wright's Jenny is the film's ghost: the character the film keeps returning to and never quite sees clearly enough. She is given a life of experience and consequences and pain that the film treats as tragedy while also using it as the backdrop against which Forrest's simplicity looks like virtue. Jenny deserves a better film than this one. She is the most interesting person in it.

The final bus stop scene, the feather rising from the road, is Zemeckis at his most confident and his most sentimental, and it earns it by being direct about what it's doing rather than sneaking the emotion in around the edges. You know exactly what the film wants you to feel. The question it leaves open is whether you trust the hand that's reaching for it.
