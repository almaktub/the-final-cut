Final Cut No. 21

Inglourious Basterds

dir. Quentin Tarantino  ·  2009  ·  Drama / War  ·  153 min
9.4
Out of ten
Tarantino playing God with history — and making the hubris feel earned.

The opening chapter — Landa and the dairy farmer — is twenty minutes of the finest scene construction in Tarantino's career, and that's saying something. Christoph Waltz is doing things with language and pleasantry that make the scene more disturbing than any amount of direct violence could. The horror is in the politeness, in the way Landa controls every turn of the conversation, and Waltz plays it with such precise delight that you feel the obscenity of the man before the film has shown you a single act of cruelty. That opening is why the film's central gamble works: by the time Tarantino rewrites history as revenge fantasy, he's already established what the fantasy is in response to.

Shosanna's story is the film's moral center and Mélanie Laurent plays it with a controlled fury that matches the material. Her face in close-up — putting on her red lipstick before the premiere, staring at her own reflection — is one of the decade's great images. The tavern scene is a masterclass in suspense through information asymmetry; the audience knows things the characters don't, the characters know things about each other that they're hiding, and Tarantino keeps every layer in the air simultaneously for twenty minutes without dropping one. The finale, the cinema burning, is cathartic in a way that doesn't pretend to be anything other than catharsis. There's no moral complexity in the ending. That's the point.

This is Tarantino at his most controlled and his most indulgent at the same time, and somehow both things serve the film. The length is earned — each chapter has its own internal logic and its own climax. The dialogue is long because the talk is where the violence lives. This is a film that understands its own genre games and plays them with full seriousness. It stays with you because it knows exactly what it's doing.
