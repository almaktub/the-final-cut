Final Cut No. 6

No Country
for Old Men

dir. Joel & Ethan Coen  ·  2007  ·  Thriller  ·  122 min
10.0
Out of ten
The Coens remove every safety net a thriller is supposed to have, and the tension doubles.

Chigurh isn't a serial killer. He's a theology. He offers his victims the coin toss because he genuinely believes he's not the one deciding — the universe is, and he's just the delivery mechanism. The film never disproves him. Carla Jean's refusal to call the toss is the closest anyone comes to beating him: she makes him own the choice, forces him to be a man rather than a mechanism, and he kills her anyway. He checks his boots on the porch after. That detail is the whole character.

The famous objections — Llewelyn dying offscreen, the quiet ending — are the two best decisions in the film. It was never about who wins the chase. It's about Bell, an old sheriff watching a world he can't read anymore, finally understanding that the violence was always here. His crippled uncle Ellis tells him about a lawman gunned down on his own porch in 1909. The title isn't a complaint about modernity. It's a diagnosis of memory. The climax happens to Bell in a chair, telling his wife about a dream of his dead father carrying fire through a cold mountain dark. Cut to black. The film hands you everything in ninety seconds and trusts you to hold it.

Bardem built the most frightening screen villain of the century out of stillness and customer-service politeness. Deakins shoots West Texas as a place that predates mercy. Carter Burwell's score is sixteen minutes of near-subliminal tones; what the film actually has is the best sound design ever put in a thriller — boot scrapes on motel carpet, the cattle gun's hiss, wind as the only witness. I've heard every case against the ending and they're all reviews of a worse movie the Coens refused to make.

"What's the most you ever lost on a coin toss?"
Anton Chigurh — No Country for Old Men
Rewatch verdict
Rewatch It
First watch is for the chase. Every one after is for Bell — the film quietly reorganizes itself around him once you know where it's going.
