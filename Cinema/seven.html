Final Cut No. 48

Se7en

dir. David Fincher  ·  1995  ·  Crime / Thriller  ·  127 min
9.0
Out of ten
A procedural so bleak it leaves a mark you can't quite wash off, and the box is the whole argument.

Fincher's city, unnamed and perpetually raining, is an environment where despair is the baseline condition, and the film's genius is that the killer's logic feels coherent within it. Not correct, but coherent. John Doe isn't random or chaotic; he has a thesis and the film has the patience to let him make it. That patience is rare in genre cinema. Most thrillers want their killers unknowable, because unknowable is scarier in the short term. Se7en understands that comprehensible is scarier in the long term: a world where someone with Doe's worldview has a point, even a broken and monstrous one, is a worse world to live in than a world where he's simply a lunatic.

Morgan Freeman's Somerset is the film's moral center: an old detective who has stopped expecting the world to be better than it is, still trying to do something good in it anyway. He reads Dante before bed. He sleeps with a metronome. He is not pessimistic so much as precisely calibrated to the reality in front of him, and that calibration has cost him something. Brad Pitt's Mills is the film's vulnerability, the person who hasn't yet arrived at Somerset's fatalism and doesn't understand why someone would want to. The antagonism between those two registers, exhausted clarity versus unbroken belief, gives the film a weight that outlasts its genre mechanics.

The ending works because Fincher and Andrew Kevin Walker understand that Somerset's final line is not despair. It's a choice made in full knowledge of what the world has just shown him. What's in the box is terrible. The film argues that the choice of how to respond to that kind of terrible is where humanity actually lives, and that the choice can go either way, and that it matters enormously which way it goes. Mills makes his choice. Somerset makes his. The film ends on Somerset walking into a field that isn't getting any brighter.

"Ernest Hemingway once wrote, 'The world is a fine place and worth fighting for.' I agree with the second part."
Somerset — Se7en
