Overall Score
"A band that had stopped speaking to each other made the most cohesive thing they'd ever made. Side B is the greatest extended musical sequence in rock."
10 because nothing on this record is wasted. Maxwell's Silver Hammer and Octopus's Garden aren't palette cleansers - they're the structural release valves that make the rest of Side A breathable and Side B's weight hit harder. Without them, Come Together through I Want You is a continuous fever pitch with no room to land. The album understood pacing the way the Beatles understood everything: intuitively, completely, and without showing the math. Side B is the greatest extended sequence in rock. The final line is either profound or manufactured and probably both. The fact that you can't tell is the point. Abbey Road is what happens when a band stops speaking and starts listening to the record they're making. It's a 10 not because it's perfect in theory but because it functions perfectly in practice.

The Farewell They Didn't Announce
Nothing on the album explicitly says this is the end. But every arrangement choice - the Suite's arc, the medley's resolution, the final 'and in the end' - reads as a conscious goodbye. They were too proud to say it directly.
🎼
Fragmentation as Form
The medley is built from songs none of them thought were complete enough to stand alone. The incompleteness is the point. Side B is what happens when you stop pretending you have to finish everything.
☀️
Beauty Without Irony
Here Comes the Sun and Something are the least guarded moments in the Beatles' catalog. No wit, no Lennon-esque deflection. Harrison earned the right to sincerity and took it.
⚡
The Weight of the Machine
Come Together opens Side A with Lennon at his most oblique. The song is about the dissolution of the band disguised as a character sketch. He always hid the personal stuff in plain sight.

The Dissection
A1 Come Together
9.6 ▼
Lennon at his most oblique. The Chuck Berry lawsuit aside, one of the most distinctive bass-led openers in rock history.
A2 Something
9.9 ▼
Harrison's best song. Sinatra called it the greatest love song of the century. He wasn't wrong. The guitar solo is the most emotionally precise playing on any Beatles record.
A3 Maxwell's Silver Hammer
7.8 ▼
Paul's music-hall impulse at its most indulgent. The other three hated recording it. You can feel that.
A4 Oh! Darling
8.7 ▼
Paul destroying his voice on purpose, recorded in one take per day for a week until it sounded sufficiently wrecked. The commitment is audible.
A5 Octopus's Garden
8 ▼
Ringo's song. Genuinely joyful in a way the album around it isn't. Earns its place as a gap in the tension.
A6 I Want You (She's So Heavy)
9.5 ▼
7:47. Three words repeated until they become abstract. Ends mid-phrase with total silence. Lennon as pure id.
B1 Here Comes the Sun
9.8 ▼
Harrison wrote it in Eric Clapton's garden to avoid a business meeting. The most purely lovely thing the Beatles ever recorded.
B2 Because
9.4 ▼
Three-part vocal harmony over a harpsichord. Lennon said the chords came from Beethoven's Moonlight Sonata played backwards. That's either true or the best liner note ever written.
B3 You Never Give Me Your Money
9.5 ▼
The medley begins. A song about money and disillusionment that pivots into a dream of escape. 'Soon we'll be away from here' lands differently when you know the context.
B4 Sun King
9 ▼
Pseudo-Spanish lyrics that mean nothing, sung as if they mean everything. The medley's breathing space.
B5 Mean Mr. Mustard
8.8 ▼
A character sketch Lennon wrote in India, folded into the Suite as connective tissue.
B6 Polythene Pam
8.9 ▼
Lennon with a Yorkshire accent, for reasons. The energy spike in the Suite before the pivot to She Came In Through the Bathroom Window.
B7 She Came In Through the Bathroom Window
9.1 ▼
A song about a fan who actually broke into Paul's house. He made it warm and slightly comic. The correct response.
B8 Golden Slumbers / Carry That Weight / The End
9.9 ▼
The climax. Paul rewrites the lullaby. The End gives every Beatle a drum solo, a guitar battle, and the last line they would ever formally record together.
B+ Her Majesty
8.5 ▼
A hidden 23-second acoustic throwaway that was accidentally included and became the most understated final track in rock history.

Footnote
They recorded Abbey Road while barely speaking. John wanted to release the Get Back sessions as the final album. Paul insisted on one more proper record. The result was better than anything either of them made alone afterward.
