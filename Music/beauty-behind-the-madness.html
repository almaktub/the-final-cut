Two of the biggest singles of 2015 on the same album. Neither one is the best track on it.

Overall Score
"The crossover that shouldn't have held together. A dark R&B record that became a pop record without becoming less dark. Tell Your Friends is the moment that makes the whole thing credible."
8.9 because The Weeknd translated his aesthetic intact to a major label release, which almost never happens. The Hills and Can't Feel My Face are both genuine artistic statements that happen to also be enormous hits - not compromises dressed up as art, but art that turned out to be commercial. Tell Your Friends is the best thing here: a single-verse demolition of everyone and everything that tried to keep him down. The Town with Drake is better than most Drake collaborations and better than most Weeknd collaborations. Dark Times with Ed Sheeran has no business working and sounds completely natural. The 1.1 missing: the middle section loses momentum. Acquainted, Same Old Song, and As You Are are all solid tracks that flatten against each other when sequenced back to back. Shameless is a fine closer but not a great one. The highs here are genuinely high. The middle is where the record breathes too easily.

The Cost of Making It
Tell Your Friends is the album's thesis statement: Abel Tesfaye addressing every person and place that shaped him, said they'd be there, and wasn't. The track lands hardest because it's not bitter - it's declarative. He made it. He's telling you. The rest of the album is what making it looks like from the inside.
🌑
Dark Pop as Subversion
Can't Feel My Face is about cocaine dependency dressed as a love song. The Hills is about anonymous sex dressed as R&B. Both tracks were inescapable radio hits in 2015. The Weeknd sold darkness to pop radio by making it impossible to dance to anything else. The listeners who heard the surface were getting something real underneath it.
💊
The Addiction Narrative
The Trilogy established the template - hedonism as survival strategy, substance use as both freedom and trap. Beauty Behind the Madness doesn't abandon that template so much as move it to a bigger stage. The same character is having the same experiences with a larger budget and a more disorienting sense of scale.
🚪
Fame as Alienation
Real Life opens the album with a man in a world that doesn't feel real. Shameless closes it with a man who has gone too far to know what real feels like anymore. In between is a record about what it costs to become famous when your identity was built on anonymity. The Weeknd was always hard to locate. Stardom made that worse.

Track by Track
The Dissection
01 Real Life
8.7 ▼
The correct opening. Disoriented from the first note. The Weeknd setting the scene before the character does anything: this world is not real, nothing here is solid. A strong opener that rewards listening to before everything that follows it.
02 Losers (feat. Labrinth)
8.6 ▼
Labrinth's production gives the track a weight that lifts it above its placement. The Weeknd and Labrinth operating on the same frequency. A collaboration that earns its feature credit.
03 Tell Your Friends
9.5 ▼
One verse. No hook in the traditional sense. Just four minutes of Abel Tesfaye stating his case with zero ambiguity. The production is the right container: all low end and pressure, nothing decorative. The best track on the album and one of the better tracks of its era.
04 The Hills
9.7 ▼
The opening line - 'I only call you when it's half past five' - sets the terms immediately. The production sounds like something going wrong in slow motion. The hook is not a hook, it's a confession delivered with the emotional detachment of someone reporting an accident. An enormous hit that operates exactly like the underground work that preceded it.
05 Acquainted
8.8 ▼
The album settling into its midsection groove. Smooth, deliberate, the production opening up after The Hills's compression. Works better as a come-down track than as a standalone.
06 The Town (feat. Drake)
9.0 ▼
Drake sounds like he cares here, which is not always guaranteed. The Weeknd gives him the right amount of space. The production underneath both of them is doing the relationship between Toronto and stardom better than either has done it alone.
07 Prisoner (feat. Lana Del Rey)
8.5 ▼
Lana Del Rey and The Weeknd occupying the same sonic and thematic space. Neither compromises. The track doesn't try to be a pop event - it's two people with the same aesthetic sharing a song. One of the more understated collabs here.
08 Same Old Song
8.4 ▼
Where the midsection starts to sag slightly. The track is good - the production is interesting and the vocal is strong - but it arrives after Acquainted and before As You Are and the three tracks blur slightly at the edges.
09 As You Are
8.3 ▼
The album's low point - not because it's weak but because it's placed between two similar tracks and can't differentiate itself fast enough. On a different album this would be a highlight.
10 Dark Times (feat. Ed Sheeran)
8.9 ▼
The most unlikely collab on the album that works completely. Ed Sheeran has no obvious business being on a Weeknd record and sounds entirely at home. The production bridges the gap between them without flattening either. A small surprise late in the album.
11 Belong to the World
8.7 ▼
The album finding its emotional register again after the midsection drift. A sample of Portishead's Machine Gun underneath, slowed and rebuilt into something unrecognizable. The Weeknd doing what he does best: taking a feeling of dissolution and making it sound good.
12 Can't Feel My Face
9.4 ▼
The pop hit that shouldn't work as a pop hit. A song about cocaine dependency that became a summer anthem because the production is too good to resist. Michael Jackson's ghost is in the groove. The irony is complete and fully intentional.
13 Shameless
8.5 ▼
The closer. Does what a closer should - lands the album rather than exploding it. The Weeknd receding into the production rather than pushing against it. Not the strongest way to end but the honest way.

Footnote
Beauty Behind the Madness debuted at number one on the Billboard 200 and became The Weeknd's commercial breakthrough after years of deliberate anonymity. He released three free mixtapes between 2011 and 2012 with no press, no interviews, and no photographs - just music uploaded online. By 2015 he was at the VMAs performing Can't Feel My Face in a red suit. The Hills and Can't Feel My Face both reached number one. The album won the Grammy for Best Urban Contemporary Album in 2016. He wore the same shade of red for the next five years.
