She made it over five years, on and off, afraid TDE would shelve it. She reportedly leaked her own title and cover art to force the release date. The album is about wanting things you shouldn't want, staying in situations you should leave, and knowing the difference while doing neither. It's 46 minutes of exact honesty. Almost no one does that.

Overall Score
"SZA gave R&B its anxiety back. Not the performed kind - the actual kind, where you know exactly what you're doing wrong and keep doing it, and are honest about both."
9.5 because Ctrl is one of the most emotionally honest albums in contemporary R&B - unflinching about insecurity, desire, self-sabotage, and the specific texture of young Black womanhood that radio had been refusing to accommodate for years. The production is beautiful and the songwriting is specific in ways that most genre writing actively avoids. The half-point it doesn't have is for a few stretches in the middle where the album loses the tension it builds so effectively in its opening run. The highs are genuine 10/10 - 20 Something alone justifies the album's existence. But the consistency wavers in a way the best records here don't. That's a real distinction, not a hedge.

The Anxiety of Wanting
Every song on Ctrl is about wanting something and being unable to fully claim it - a person, a version of yourself, a clean ending. SZA doesn't want from a position of strength. She wants from a position of knowing she shouldn't, which is the honest position, which is the one that got left out of R&B for years. The control the album is named for is something she doesn't have. That's the whole album.
🪞
Insecurity as Honesty
Drew Barrymore opens with SZA wondering if she's too weird, too much, not enough, why he'd want someone like her. This is not false modesty. It's the specific internal monologue that most pop music aestheticizes into power moves instead of letting breathe. SZA lets it breathe. She stays with the discomfort past the point where most singers would pivot to a chorus that resolves things. She doesn't resolve things. Things stay unresolved. That's what makes it true.
🔥
Self-Destruction as Self-Knowledge
Supermodel opens the album with SZA admitting she cheated, drove drunk, made bad decisions - and doing it with the cadence of someone who has examined those choices clearly enough to describe them exactly. This is not glorification. It's autopsy. She knows why she did it, what it cost, and what it says about where she was. The difference between romanticizing self-destruction and understanding it is precision. Ctrl has precision.
🌺
Black Femininity Without Filter
The album covers the full range of what it's like to be a young Black woman wanting - wanting love, respect, a body you're comfortable in, a relationship that doesn't require you to shrink. Garden (Say It Like Dat) is the most understated love song on the record and one of the best of its decade. 20 Something closes the album by refusing to be reassuring about what getting older means. It ends on uncertainty, which is the only honest place to end.

Track by Track
The Dissection
01 Supermodel
9.7 ▼
The opening track and the album's most confrontational. SZA admits to cheating, to leaving, to making the worst choice available - and the production is so warm and enveloping that the confession lands as relief rather than confession. The arrangement sounds like summer through a window, which creates the dissonance: she's telling you something dark in a voice that sounds like comfort. That is the album's central technical achievement, established in the first three minutes.
02 Love Galore (feat. Travis Scott)
9.3 ▼
The dismissal. SZA is done with the relationship and the song is a structured goodbye - polite, firm, slightly too articulate to be fully believed. Travis Scott's verse works precisely because it doesn't try to match her register. He sounds like the other person in the room who has already accepted this is over. The production is sparse enough to keep the focus on the exchange, not the atmosphere.
04 Drew Barrymore
9.8 ▼
The album's emotional center and its most vulnerable track. SZA wonders whether she's too strange for the person she's with - whether she should be more like Drew Barrymore, who she associates with a more legible, easier femininity. It's the most specific expression of the album's core anxiety: the fear that who you actually are is incompatible with being loved. The song doesn't resolve this. It stays in the question. That is why it works.
07 Broken Clocks
9.5 ▼
Allegedly about working at TDE while her career stalled - the grind without guarantee, the faith without evidence. The production swells in ways that feel earned by the lyric, not decorative. SZA's voice is more controlled here than anywhere else on the record. She is presenting the argument rather than feeling it in real time, which is a different kind of technique and she handles both modes well.
10 Garden (Say It Like Dat)
9.6 ▼
The album's quietest and most complete love song. SZA asks for small things - say it the way you say it, be the version of yourself that you are with me when we're alone. The request is not grand. It is specific. The song understands that intimacy is in the specifics - in the private language two people develop that means nothing to anyone outside it. She is asking him to keep speaking hers. The guitar is everything.
14 20 Something
9.9 ▼
The closing track and the only song on the album where SZA sounds like she's addressing future-herself rather than current-herself. She is 20-something - old enough to know she should have things figured out, young enough that she still doesn't, aware of both conditions simultaneously. The production drops to almost nothing. Just her voice and a few notes. "Still a little lost and I can't fake it." The album ends without resolution because there isn't one yet. This is the most honest thing a closing track can do.

Footnote
SZA reportedly leaked the album title and cover art herself when she became afraid TDE would shelve the project or keep delaying the release. The label confirmed a date shortly after. The word "Ctrl" refers to the control she felt she lacked - over her career, over her relationships, over the version of herself that gets presented to the world. The title is the argument. She named the problem before anyone could name it for her.
