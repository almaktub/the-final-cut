Overall Score
"741 weeks on the Billboard 200. Not because it's comfortable - because it's true."
10 because this is the most unified album in rock. Every element - the heartbeat that opens and closes it, the cash register sounds, the clocks, Clare Torry's scream on The Great Gig in the Sky - exists in service of the same argument. The album is about the passage of time and the things that go wrong inside it: money, madness, violence, age, death. It doesn't comfort you. It names what's happening to you. Any Colour You Like is a corridor, not a room - but even perfect buildings have corridors, and this one connects two of the album's best moments without wasting a second. 741 weeks on the Billboard 200 is not a stat about popularity. It's a stat about how many people heard it and felt correctly diagnosed.
Central Thesis
What This Album Is Really About
Time as the Enemy Money as Corruption Madness as the Honest Response Death as the Frame
⏰
Time as the Enemy
The album is a clock. It opens with a heartbeat and ends with one. Time begins the subject of the fourth track and never stops being the subject. 'No one told you when to run - you missed the starting gun.' The horror is that this is true of everyone and no one does anything about it.
💰
Money as Corruption
The cash register sounds that open Money are cut from real register tape. Roger Waters made the loop himself. The joke is that the most commercial song on the album about money is the one that begins with the sound of a transaction. The band knew exactly what they were doing.
🌀
Madness as the Honest Response
Brain Damage is about Syd Barrett, the original Floyd frontman who lost his mind and left. But the album frames madness not as failure but as a possible response to an insane world. The lunatic is on the grass. So are you. What's the difference, exactly?
🌑
Death as the Frame
The dark side of the moon is where the sun never shines. Eclipse - the closer - lists everything humans do, everything life contains, and then notes that all of it is eclipsed by the moon. The heartbeat returns and you realize the whole album has been bounded by a single human lifetime.
"And everything under the sun is in tune, but the sun is eclipsed by the moon.
Roger Waters · Eclipse · 1973
Track by Track
The Dissection
01Speak to Me / Breathe
9.4▼
The heartbeat, the cash register, the scream, the laughter - all the album's sounds previewed in sixty seconds, then Breathe drops in like a sigh. 'Breathe, breathe in the air. Don't be afraid to care.' The most gentle opening to an album about mortality you'll find.
03Time
9.8▼
Opens with an alarm clock ambush - every clock in the studio, triggered at once. Then: 'Ticking away the moments that make up a dull day.' The opening verse is the most precise description of wasted time ever set to a rock beat. By the time 'the time is gone, the song is over' arrives, it actually is.
04The Great Gig in the Sky
9.9▼
Clare Torry was a session singer brought in to improvise over a chord sequence. She was told the track was about death. What she produced - that wordless wail, rising and falling - is the most visceral rendering of mortal terror in rock. She was paid £30 session fee. She later sued and received a songwriting credit. Both things feel correct.
05Money
9.6▼
7/4 time signature - the cash register loop is in 7/4 and the whole song follows. The riff is one of the most recognizable in rock and was written by Roger Waters on a bass guitar he didn't know how to play. The saxophone solo in the middle section is the one time the album lets itself get greedy, and it's the point.
07Brain Damage
9.7▼
A love letter to Syd Barrett that's also a self-portrait. 'I'll see you on the dark side of the moon' - addressed to the man who went there first. The album title is delivered here and it lands like a destination the whole record has been heading toward.
08Eclipse
9.8▼
Thirteen lines. Everything a human being can do listed without judgment, then eclipsed. The heartbeat returns. An old man's voice says 'There is no dark side of the moon, really. Matter of fact, it's all dark.' The album ends before you're ready for it to.

Footnote
On the day the album was being mixed at Abbey Road, Syd Barrett - the founding member the album is partly about - walked into the studio unannounced. He was unrecognizable: shaved head, shaved eyebrows, overweight. The band didn't know who he was at first. Roger Waters allegedly cried. Barrett never recorded again. The album was released three weeks later.
