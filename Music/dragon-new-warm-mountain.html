Overall Score
"Twenty songs about the body in the world - the body in love, in grief, in soil, in time - from a band that treats folk music like it still has something new to say about all four."
9.1 because this is the rare double album that earns its length. Most doubles are a strong album padded into two; Dragon New Warm Mountain is an argument that a single disc would have been the wrong container for what they were trying to hold. Adrianne Lenker's writing is at its most confident here - she trusts the mundane detail to carry enormous weight and it does, track after track. Simulation Swarm is the one that will follow the album into whatever's next: a folk song that sounds like it's always been there, the way the best folk songs do. The 0.9: the album's final third asks more patience than the opening earned you credit for. Not filler - but thinner.

What This Album Is Really About

🌿
Grief as Natural Process
Loss here isn't dramatic - it's seasonal. Things end. Things come back in different forms. Lenker writes about death and separation the way you'd write about soil: with curiosity instead of despair. The album makes grief feel less like a wound and more like weather.
🏔️
The Body in the Land
Physical reality - roots, rivers, mountains, animals - is never metaphor here. It's the actual subject. Big Thief writes about the natural world because they believe it has something to say about human experience, not as backdrop but as participant.
🏡
Memory as Architecture
The album is built from specific remembered places and people. Lenker treats memory not as nostalgia but as load-bearing structure - the past isn't something you visit, it's something you live inside of while trying to move forward. Canada captures this most directly.
🎸
Song as Sacred Object
They recorded in four different locations - a cabin, a studio, a country house, a practice space - and let each environment shape the sound. The album treats the act of recording as something close to ritual: a way of preserving what's alive before it changes.

Track by Track
The Dissection
01Change
9.3▼
A statement of purpose that works as a complete song. The title is not ironic - the album is genuinely about change as process, and this opener lays the emotional key without announcing itself as an opener.
02Sparrow
8.9▼
A small song about a small thing that somehow opens onto an enormous feeling. Lenker writes birds the way other songwriters write cities: as specific, mortal, worth paying attention to.
03Dried Roses
9.0▼
A relationship autopsy conducted with more generosity than most. Lenker doesn't assign blame - she just describes what was. The guitar line carries grief that the lyrics refuse to perform.
04Candle
8.7▼
A quieter mid-section moment. The image of the candle earns its metaphorical weight by being handled so plainly that it stops feeling like one.
05No Reason
8.8▼
A song about the inexplicability of love that doesn't mistake the inexplicability for romance. Good things happen without reason. Bad things happen without reason. The album holds both.
06Wake Me Up to Drive
8.6▼
A road song about someone who trusts you enough to sleep in the passenger seat. That trust as a form of love is the whole idea, and it lands.
07Spud Infinity
8.5▼
A playful track that sounds effortless in the best way. The title is absurd on purpose. The song earns the absurdity by being genuinely joyful rather than performing it.
08Simulation Swarm
9.5▼
The album's peak and one of the decade's finest folk songs. The melody is old in a way that good melodies sometimes are - it sounds discovered, not written. 'I am not afraid to die, but I am afraid of time' is a line that will outlast everything around it.
09Canada
9.0▼
A specific place remembered with the kind of precision that makes strangers homesick for somewhere they've never been. The geography is real. The feeling it carries is universal.
10Bluebird
9.2▼
One of the album's most melodically complete tracks. The band at their most traditionally beautiful, and the arrangement has enough restraint that the emotion earns its moment.
11Certainty
8.8▼
Certainty treated not as comfort but as another form of limitation. To know something absolutely is to stop asking. Lenker seems suspicious of certainty throughout this album. This track makes the case most directly.
12Little Things Left Behind
8.4▼
Where the album begins to thin slightly. Still good. A song about aftermath - what remains when people leave. The concept is strong; the execution is reliable rather than inspired.
13Time Escaping
8.7▼
A title that describes both the subject and the feeling of listening to a double album by this point. The track earns its place by being genuinely anxious rather than philosophically anxious.
14The Only Place
8.6▼
An arrival song. Someone found where they're supposed to be. The restraint in the production - the way the band holds back even as the lyrics arrive at something - is exactly right.
15Flower of Blood
8.9▼
One of the late-album tracks that justifies the length. The imagery is stranger than most of the record - darker, more bodily - and the song earns the strangeness without explaining it.
16Real Love
8.5▼
A direct track that says what it means. Refreshing in the context of an album this poetic. Not every song needs to be elliptical. This one works by being plain.
17Twelve
8.4▼
A childhood memory that doesn't over-explain its emotional weight. The specific age locates a feeling without narrating it. Minor but clean.
18Enemy
8.7▼
An uncomfortable track that earns its discomfort. Lenker doesn't sanitize the parts of human experience that don't resolve. The enemy in the song might be a person or might be something internal. The ambiguity is the point.
19Red Moon
9.1▼
The album finds a second wind here. Darker and stranger than the earlier material, and better for it. A penultimate track that makes the album feel like it had somewhere left to go.
20Promise Is a Pendulum
8.8▼
A closing track that understands its job: not to resolve but to continue. The pendulum is still swinging when the album ends. That's the only honest place to stop.

Footnote
Recorded in four different spaces - a cabin in upstate New York, a studio in Los Angeles, a country house in Massachusetts, and a practice space in Topanga - over the course of pandemic restrictions. The four environments are audible in the album's tonal variety. They weren't trying for consistency. That turned out to be the right call.
