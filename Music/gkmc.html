A short film disguised as a rap album. Seventeen-year-old Kendrick narrates the worst night of his life. Every voicemail is a scene break. Every skit is load-bearing.
0.0
Overall Score
"The most cinematic rap album ever made. Not as a metaphor - literally structured like a film, with a protagonist, a night that goes wrong, and a character who is not the same person at the end that he was at the beginning."
9.8 because the album is so deliberately narrative that the music occasionally serves the story more than it serves itself. That's the right trade-off given what it's trying to do - this is a film score for a movie only you can see - but it means individual tracks carry slightly less weight in isolation than on TPAB. The exception is Backseat Freestyle, which could appear on any rap album and still be the best thing on it. The interludes are not filler. The voicemails are not skits. The moment his mother calls and says "be safe" is the emotional center of a 68-minute record and it lasts twelve seconds. That's construction.

🌆
The City as Character
Compton isn't a backdrop. It's a force that makes decisions for the people living in it. The "m.A.A.d city" half of the title isn't description - it's indictment. The city has its own agenda and the protagonist is a minor obstacle in it.
📱
The Voicemail as Narration
His parents' calls aren't skits. They're the film's third-person narration - the world outside Kendrick's own perspective, calling in to check on a story he's in the middle of. The gap between what his mother says and what he's actually doing is the whole album's tension.
🚗
Knowing and Doing Anyway
Every bad decision in the album is made by someone who already knows it's a bad decision. The album isn't about ignorance - it's about the specific gravity of a place that pulls you toward choices you've already argued against in your own head.
🌙
The Night That Costs You
The 17-year-old at the start of the album is gone by the end. The record tracks a specific window - one night - where something irreversible happens to who a person is. Not death. Something subtler. The loss of a version of yourself.

Track by Track
The Dissection
01 Sherane a.k.a Master Splinter's Daughter
8.8 ▼
The film starts mid-action. You're already in the car, already headed somewhere you shouldn't be. The beat is slow and summer-heavy. Everything that follows happens because of this one decision.
02 Bitch, Don't Kill My Vibe
9.0 ▼
The one track that could exist outside the album's narrative and still be essential. The beat breathes. Kendrick's tone is already somewhere else - not aggressive, just very far away from wherever you are.
03 Backseat Freestyle
9.8 ▼
Pure id. Pure youth. The album needs this because every other track has already grown up. This is what 17 sounds like before the night goes sideways - unguarded, loud, totally unconcerned with consequences. The joy makes what follows harder to take.
04 The Art of Peer Pressure
9.2 ▼
Six men in a car making decisions. The grammar shifts to first person plural and never fully shifts back. "We" as the structure of complicity - no single person responsible, everyone responsible. The most politically precise track on the record and it sounds like a party.
05 Money Trees (feat. Jay Rock)
9.7 ▼
Jay Rock shows up and earns every second. The beat is one of the most beautiful things in West Coast rap - lazy and dangerous at the same time. The closing notes are perfect. "Halle Berry or hallelujah" is the album's single best line.
06 Poetic Justice (feat. Drake)
8.5 ▼
The album's breathing room. Drake is exactly as useful here as he needs to be - which is not very, but appropriately so. The track exists to give the narrative space before it gets serious. It works.
07 good kid
9.1 ▼
The title. The thesis. The character's honest self-assessment in the middle of everything that's already happened. "I am a good kid" delivered from inside a situation that is testing that claim in real time.
08 m.A.A.d city (feat. MC Eiht)
9.7 ▼
MC Eiht appears and the 90s weight lands on the track. The production breaks twice - the second half is scarier than the first. The drums on the back end are not a sample. They're a heartbeat at the wrong speed.
09 Swimming Pools (Drank)
9.3 ▼
What sounds like a party track is an anxiety attack dressed as an invitation. The layered voices are Kendrick arguing with himself. The hook is seductive specifically so that the verses can demonstrate what the hook costs.
10 Sing About Me, I'm Dying of Thirst
9.9 ▼
Twelve minutes. Two movements. The pivot from mortality to prayer is not sentimental - it's earned across eleven minutes of accumulation. The best track on the album. The verse from the sister of the man killed in Section.80 is the moment everything becomes real.
11 Real (feat. Anna Wise)
8.7 ▼
The denouement. His parents' voices return. The album has been a single night and the morning has arrived. The question the track asks - what is real - has already been answered by everything before it.
12 Compton (feat. Dr. Dre)
8.6 ▼
Dr. Dre appears for the outro and the film rolls credits. The only track that looks outward rather than in. The city gets the last word, which is the correct ending - this was always its story more than Kendrick's.

Footnote
The album was recorded largely in the house Kendrick grew up in. The voicemails are from his actual parents. The characters in the interludes are based on real people from Compton. The fiction and the autobiography are the same document.
