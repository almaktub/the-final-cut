His first album made after becoming famous - which means it's his first album made with something to lose. The divide is geographic, generational, and psychic. You can't go home if the person who left is not the person who returns.

Overall Score
"The superstar's dilemma, Vermont edition: the place that made you can't hold you, and you can't stop reaching back for it."
8.3 because the best tracks here - "All Them Horses," "The Great Divide," "Porch Light," "Dan" - rank among the sharpest writing Kahan has done. The album earns its scale. The deduction is for a back half that loses momentum in spots ("Willing and Able," "Spoiled") where the production reaches for grandeur the songs haven't quite earned. The three or four best tracks are career-best. The rest is very good. A record that will only improve with time and context.
Central Thesis
What This Album Is Really About
The Guilt of Leaving Vermont as Relationship Fame as Distance Family as Anchor and Weight
🏔️
The Guilt of Leaving
Kahan was on tour in Europe when the 2023 Vermont floods hit - the worst in the state since 1927. He couldn't get home. "All Them Horses" is that song: the guilt of absence, the helplessness of distance, the particular shame of being somewhere beautiful when the people you love are underwater.
🌲
Vermont as Relationship
Not a backdrop. A person you are in a complicated long-distance relationship with. The album is full of this - he's addressing Vermont the way you address a partner you've grown away from. The state has opinions about him. He has guilt about the state. It's mutual.
📺
Fame as Distance
Stick Season made him a phenomenon. The Great Divide is the first album written from the other side of that - after the late nights on Fallon, after the festival headlining, after extended time in LA. The divide is partly what celebrity does to the relationship between where you came from and who you became.
🪔
Family as Anchor and Weight
"Porch Light" and "Dan" are the album's most intimate moments - direct address to specific people in his life, not to Vermont as concept. These tracks work because they don't generalize. They're particular in the way the best folk writing is particular: you know who he's talking to even though you don't know them.
"I stare across it. I see old friends, my father, my mother, my siblings, my younger self, the great state of Vermont.

Rare Frequencies
Things You Didn't Know
01
The Album That Almost Wasn't Called This
The album was almost titled The Last of the Bugs, taken from a line in "The View Between Villages" off Stick Season. Kahan ran a mysterious TikTok account with that name for months, dropping clues - fans pieced it together before any announcement. He ultimately felt it was too esoteric for an album title. He kept it for the expanded edition instead.
02
The Vermont Floods
July 2023: Vermont flooded. Roads washed out. Bridges collapsed. Kahan was mid-tour in Europe. He watched it on his phone from a hotel in another country, unable to get home, watching his neighbors wade through their living rooms. "All Them Horses" is the direct result. He wrote it quickly, almost compulsively. It shows.
03
Number One, Both Sides
The Great Divide debuted at #1 in both the US and UK - the best first-week sales for a rock album in the US in over a decade. This from a singer-songwriter from Vermont who got famous on TikTok with songs about not being able to leave. He left. It went to number one. He still can't stop writing about leaving.
Track by Track
The Dissection
01End of August
8.5▼
The album's opening move - setting the season, the temperature, the emotional register. August in Vermont means summer is almost over, school is coming, something is ending. Kahan uses the month not as a date but as a feeling. A clean, precise opener that doesn't oversell what follows.
06The Great Divide
9.0▼
The thesis track and the album's first genuinely large moment. The production here earns the space it takes up. The divide he's singing about is not metaphor dressed up as landscape - it's the actual psychic gap between who he was in Vermont and who he became on the road. The chorus lands because the verses build honestly toward it.
11Porch Light
8.8▼
A direct address to his parents. Specific in the way great folk writing is specific - you don't need to know who he's talking to, but you feel the weight of someone who does. The porch light as the thing that stays on when you don't come home. Simple image. Earns every syllable.
16All Them Horses
9.3▼
The album's best song. Written about the 2023 Vermont floods - he was in Europe, watching from a hotel, unable to get home. The guilt of having left is everywhere in this record, but here it becomes acute, particular, unmistakably real. The image of horses specifically - animals that couldn't evacuate themselves - lands with the weight of the actual thing that happened.
17Dan
8.7▼
Named after a specific person. Closes the album. Whatever distance the record opened up, this track attempts the crossing - not successfully, necessarily, but honestly. An ending that doesn't resolve everything because not everything resolves. The correct call.

Footnote
Kahan said the album title came from an image he kept returning to: standing at the edge of a vast distance, looking across at the people and places he came from. The divide is not a metaphor for anything. It is the actual feeling of leaving a place that formed you and watching it get smaller in the rearview and knowing you made the choice to drive.
