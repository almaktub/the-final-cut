Album Autopsy · 048
Lana Del Rey
Honeymoon
Interscope · 2015 · Dream Pop / Indie
The cinematic sorrow of a woman who has made peace with not escaping
0.0
Her most interior record - built slow, arranged like a score, and deliberately unapproachable for anyone who wants a hit.
Honeymoon came after the backlash of Born to Die and the more defensive Ultraviolence, and it sounds like a record made by someone who has stopped caring about the conversation. It's the slowest Lana album, the most orchestrated, the least concessive. Eleven tracks that move at the tempo of an afternoon you can't remember ending. The Old Hollywood references aren't ironic - she means them, and meaning them completely is what makes it strange and beautiful. It's an album about loving the thing that's consuming you, which describes both her subject matter and her artistic method.
Recurring Themes
🎬
Melancholy as Aesthetic and Confession
The record refuses to distinguish between genuine sorrow and performed sorrow - and eventually you realize the distinction doesn't matter. If the pose becomes permanent enough, it becomes the person. Lana understood this before her critics did.
🌴
California Gothic
The California of Honeymoon is not the California of surf rock or tech optimism. It's the California of faded glamour and highway darkness - the state as a place where beautiful things come to decay slowly in the sun.
🔥
Loving What Destroys You
The relationship songs here aren't about leaving or being left - they're about choosing to stay with full knowledge of the cost. "The Blackest Day" is the most explicit, but the whole record hums with the quiet logic of someone who would rather burn than be cold.
🌙
Old Hollywood as Haunting
She quotes Nina Simone, namechecks a 1940s actor, builds entire songs around a vintage aesthetic that isn't nostalgic so much as hungry. The past keeps reaching through the record because the present doesn't offer the same quality of doom.
"Will you still love me when I'm no longer young and beautiful?"

Easter Eggs & Annotations
Cover Version
The Nina Simone Cover
Her version of "Don't Let Me Be Misunderstood" - a song Simone made devastating and the Animals made famous - is one of the few cases where Lana's version doesn't feel secondary. She slows it further and makes the desperation operatic.
After the Backlash
Defiance Through Slowness
Born to Die made her a target. Ultraviolence was louder. Honeymoon is quieter than either, and the quiet is the answer - not defensive, just entirely indifferent to criticism. The album's pace is a position.
Film Score
Orchestral Architecture
The string arrangements on Honeymoon were built track-by-track with conductor Gillian Welch. They don't accompany the songs - they are the songs. Remove them and you have skeletal demos; with them you have something that sounds like a 1960s Italian film score.
Reference
Terrence Malick and Malick Logic
The album moves the way Malick films move: not by plot or progression but by image and feeling. "Music to Watch Boys To" is not about a narrative - it's about the quality of attention. Lana's cinema influences aren't decoration; they're structural.
Track-by-Track
Honeymoon
9.3
The title track opens the record and lays out everything that follows in one long, slow exhale. The orchestration is lush without being overwrought; the vocal is Lana at her most controlled. "We both know that it's not fashionable to love me" is the album's thesis line - loving what the world has decided not to love. The seven-minute runtime doesn't drag because it was never trying to arrive anywhere.
The Blackest Day
9.2
The most emotionally bare track on the album. Where the other songs wear their sorrow like a garment, "The Blackest Day" wears it like a wound. The production strips back and what's left is a vocal that doesn't perform grief - it reports it. The bass drone underneath is oppressive in the right way.
Terrence Loves You
9.0
Built around a David Bowie sample ("Space Oddity") that she uses so lightly you might miss it. The song is about distance - not geographic distance but the specific distance of feeling someone slipping from you while they're still in the room. Her vocal delivery here is one of her finest.
Music to Watch Boys To
8.8
The title is slightly absurd and completely right. The song exists in a particular mode of passive watching - not desire, not pursuit, just observation of beautiful people being beautiful. It's a strange, specific feeling and she captures it exactly. The production is minimal enough to feel like a thought.
Don't Let Me Be Misunderstood
8.7
The Nina Simone cover lands in a specific context: after two albums of being systematically misread by critics who couldn't separate the persona from the person, covering a song literally titled "Don't Let Me Be Misunderstood" is either the most or least subtle thing she's ever done. The performance suggests she knows.
High By the Beach
8.5
The most commercially viable thing on the record, which makes it the odd one out - a genuine pop song dropped into a film score. Its presence makes more sense as a structural break than a standalone track. Still: the hook is there, the production is lean, and she sings it like she doesn't care that it's the single, which is probably why it works.

Honeymoon is the Lana album that most rewards patience and most punishes impatience. Put it on as background music and it's pleasant. Sit with it and give it your full attention and by "The Blackest Day" you'll understand what she was doing - not performing sadness for an audience, but building an exact replica of a very particular interior state and inviting you to live in it for an hour. The invitation is the point.
