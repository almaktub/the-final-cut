Album Autopsy · 058
Tyler, the Creator
Igor
Columbia · 2019 · Neo-Soul / Art Pop
Igor is the obsessive, the ex is the object - Tyler wrote both of them and is pretending to be neither
0.0
A breakup album told through a character who doesn't realize he's the villain - which is the most honest way to tell it.
Igor is a concept album where the concept is jealousy: its logic, its ugliness, its confusion with love. Tyler plays a character who is obsessed with someone who has moved on, who refuses to accept it, whose devotion curdles into something controlling. The album doesn't editorialize - it inhabits the perspective so fully that you feel the distortion from inside it. The production is immaculate: neo-soul textures, dense chord work, samples so transformed they become new things. Tyler played almost everything himself. The Grammy for Best Rap Album was bewildering to anyone who listened to the record, which is barely a rap album at all - and is all the better for it. "Gone Gone / Thank You" is the turning point: the moment the obsession finally lets go, and the relief and grief arrive simultaneously.
Recurring Themes
🎭
The Mask as Truth-Telling Device
Igor is a character - bowl cut, ill-fitting suit, blank expression. But the character says things Tyler can't say directly. The mask isn't hiding anything; it's creating the distance required to be fully honest. The character and the composer are the same person operating at different temperatures.
🔪
Obsession and Its Violence
"New Magic Wand" is a song about wanting to kill your ex's new partner - performed with such pop brightness that the horror sneaks up on you. The album understands that obsessive love contains violence, and it refuses to romanticize this. Igor is not a good person. That's the whole point.
💜
Queerness Without Declaration
Igor addresses a man throughout. Tyler never explains or annotates this. The album simply proceeds as if queer love is as available for pop operatics as any other kind - which it is, and which most of the genre had spent decades pretending it wasn't.
🌅
Grief as Maturation
The album ends with acceptance - not happy, but honest. "I think I'm falling apart but that's okay" is where Igor lands. The character who couldn't let go finally does. What he discovers on the other side is not joy but clarity. That's a more valuable thing to find.

Easter Eggs & Annotations
The Instruments
Tyler Played Almost Everything
Igor's dense, layered production - the chord stabs, the bass lines, the key textures - was played almost entirely by Tyler in his own studio. The album sounds like a full ensemble because he built each part individually. The control is total, which is why the loss of control in the lyrics lands so hard by contrast.
The Grammy
Best Rap Album, Not a Rap Album
Igor won Best Rap Album at the 2020 Grammys. Tyler's acceptance speech addressed this directly: "I feel like it's a backhanded compliment. When we - and I mean guys that look like me - do anything that's genre-bending or that's weird or different, automatically it's put in a rap or urban category." The category problem was the critique.
The Cameos
Solange and Carti, Uncredited
Solange Knowles appears on "Earfquake" and Playboi Carti appears on "Earfquake" and "I Think" - both uncredited on the original release. Their appearances were confirmed later. The decision to leave credits off was deliberate: Igor is a singular voice and the collaborators serve that voice rather than announcing themselves.
The Arc
From Goblin to Igor
Tyler's debut Goblin (2011) was largely performed as a disturbed adolescent persona - shock, aggression, transgression as its own end. Igor (2019) is the emotional inverse: interior, vulnerable, formally sophisticated. The eight years between them represent one of the most complete artistic evolutions in contemporary music.
Track-by-Track
Gone Gone / Thank You
9.9
The album's turning point and its best song. The first half - "Gone Gone" - is Igor still in the grip of obsession, desperate and dissonant. The second half - "Thank You" - is the release. The production shift between them is one of the most emotionally precise production decisions in recent pop: everything opens up, breathes, lets in light. The character finally lets go. The listener exhales with him.
Earfquake
9.7
The album's most immediately accessible track and still one of its best. Carti's uncredited vocal is the perfect foil - his detached, almost bored delivery against Tyler's yearning is the album's central dynamic compressed into a single song. "Please don't say you love me" is a request so tangled in its own contradictions that only a completely honest lyricist could write it without irony.
New Magic Wand
9.5
The darkest track on the record and the one that most clearly positions Igor as an unreliable narrator. The impulse - to eliminate the rival - is rendered with enough pop sheen that you might miss the horror on first listen. On second listen, the brightness of the production makes the content more disturbing, not less. Tyler earns the discomfort.
Puppet
9.4
Kanye West's uncredited appearance in the final minute recontextualizes everything. Two artists at very different points in their relationship with obsession occupy the same track. Tyler's verse is confused and reaching; Kanye's arrival feels like a ghost from a future Tyler might become. The song doesn't explain itself. It expects you to feel the weight without commentary.
What's Good
9.2
The confrontation scene. After an album of obsession and avoidance, Igor finally addresses the ex directly - and the production responds by becoming the most aggressive thing on the record. The release valve. The anger that's been sublimated into production and character all album long finally surfaces. It's uglier and more honest than everything before it.

Are We Still Friends?
8.7
Igor is what happens when a rapper decides he's done performing toughness and starts performing truth instead. Tyler built a character to create distance and ended up using that distance to say the most direct things he's ever said. The irony is that Igor - this invented, masked persona - is more recognizably human than almost anything Tyler released under his own name. The character study is a self-portrait. The mask was a mirror all along.
