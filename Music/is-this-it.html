Album Autopsy · 061
The Strokes
Is This It
RCA · 2001 · Indie Rock / Post-Punk Revival
Five rich New York kids made the most important rock album of the 2000s by making it sound like they weren't trying - the effort was enormous
0.0
The album that saved guitar rock in 2001 by making guitar rock sound like it didn't need saving - effortless on the surface, meticulously constructed underneath.
Is This It arrived at the precise moment when rock music needed someone to prove it could still be cool without being heavy, cerebral without being pretentious, influenced without being derivative. The Strokes were the answer to a question the industry didn't know it was asking. Julian Casablancas' vocal - recorded through a guitar amp to create the lo-fi warmth - sounds like someone singing into a tin can in the best possible way: close, imperfect, present. The twin guitars of Albert Hammond Jr. and Nick Valensi interlock with a precision that disguises how much practice it took to sound this casual. Thirty-three minutes. Eleven songs. No wasted space. The sonic template for an entire decade of indie rock, deployed once and then immediately impossible to repeat.
Recurring Themes
🗽
New York as Character
The album is saturated with a specific Manhattan - downtown, late night, apartments too small for the feelings happening inside them. The city isn't backdrop; it's emotional weather. The Strokes didn't invent New York rock, but Is This It defined what it sounded like for a generation who grew up after the Velvet Underground.
😶
Cool as Defense Mechanism
Casablancas sings with almost no emotional affect - the boredom is the performance, and the performance masks the feeling. But the feeling is there: under every detached lyric is a person who wants something they won't admit to wanting. The gap between the cool exterior and the longing interior is where every song on the album lives.
🌙
The Night as Emotional Terrain
Is This It is a nocturnal album - bars, cab rides, apartments at 3am, the particular exhaustion of wanting to go home and not knowing where home is. The production (warm, slightly degraded, intimate) sounds like the inside of a night that's going on too long and you're starting to enjoy it anyway.
🎸
The Performance of Effortlessness
The album's greatest trick: it sounds like five guys who stumbled into a studio on a Tuesday afternoon. It was not that. The guitar arrangements are mathematically precise. The tempos are locked. The production was obsessively refined. The effortlessness is the hardest thing to make and the thing you can't hear without knowing what to listen for.
"I said please don't slow me down / If I'm going too fast / You're in a hurry / I'm not looking at the past."

Easter Eggs & Annotations
The Vocal
Singing Through a Guitar Amp
Producer Gordon Raphael recorded Casablancas' vocals through a guitar amplifier to create the lo-fi, slightly distorted texture that defines the album's sound. The technique was practical - they were working in a small New York studio with limited equipment - and became definitional. Hundreds of indie bands spent the next decade trying to replicate the accident.
The Removal
New York City Cops Pulled After 9/11
"New York City Cops" - with its chorus "New York City cops / They ain't too smart" - was pulled from the US version of the album after September 11, 2001. It appeared on the UK version. The gap in the US tracklist was filled by "When It Started." The removed track is better than its replacement and became more sought-after precisely because of its absence.
The School
Casablancas and Hammond, Since Boarding School
Julian Casablancas and Albert Hammond Jr. met at Institut Le Rosey, a Swiss boarding school that charges approximately $130,000 a year in tuition. The Strokes' image as scruffy New York underground heroes was always complicated by this biography. The album's themes of ennui and boredom land differently when you know how much the ennui cost.
The Influence
What It Unlocked
Is This It is one of the most directly influential rock albums of the 21st century - Arctic Monkeys, Interpol, Franz Ferdinand, Bloc Party, countless others cite it as the record that made them believe guitar bands were still possible and necessary. The debt is rarely proportional to the acknowledgment. It usually isn't.
Track-by-Track
Last Nite
9.7
The album's signature track and the song that most cleanly demonstrates its method: a riff so simple it sounds inevitable, a lyric so specific it could only be about one situation, an arrangement so locked it feels like it was always going to exist. The guitar figure is lifted (or heavily borrowed) from Tom Petty's "American Girl." Petty was reportedly fine with it. The song is a masterpiece regardless of provenance.
Hard to Explain
9.6
The album's best track and the one that most clearly shows what the Strokes were capable of beyond the immediate appeal of the debut. The chorus riff is one of the best in their catalog. Casablancas' lyric - about the difficulty of articulating something you feel but can't justify - is the album's emotional thesis in three minutes and twenty seconds.
Someday
9.5
The most melodically generous track on the record. The guitar line is immediately memorable; the song arrives at its hook within the first thirty seconds and earns it. "In many ways they'll miss the good old days / Someday, someday" - the nostalgia for the present tense, the mourning that precedes the loss, is the album's most clearly articulated emotional position.
Barely Legal
9.3
The opener and the fastest thing on the album. Casablancas sounds most energized here - not bored, not detached, genuinely urgent. The title is more provocative than the content warrants, which is typical: Is This It names things in ways that suggest more transgression than they deliver, and is more interesting for the gap between the name and the thing.
The Modern Age
9.2
The track that broke the band - released as an independent single before any label involvement, it spread through music blogs and UK press at a speed that made record executives feel they were late to something that had already happened. The song itself is perfectly calibrated: it announces the album's aesthetic in three minutes and leaves you needing the other ten tracks immediately.

Take It or Leave It
8.5
The question the album's title asks - is this it? - was answered by the decade of music it produced. Yes, for a lot of bands, this was it: the template, the aspiration, the thing to be measured against. The Strokes themselves never equaled it, which is either the tragedy of their story or the proof of how good the debut was. Probably both. Is This It is thirty-three minutes of a band operating at a level they had no business being at on their first album, and which they spent the rest of their career acknowledging, in various ways, that they'd already peaked.
