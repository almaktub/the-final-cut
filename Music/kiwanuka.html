His name is the whole album title. Not Michael Kiwanuka. Just Kiwanuka. That decision is the record's opening argument - and everything that follows is the defense.

Overall Score
"Where Love & Hate was about searching for a place to belong, Kiwanuka is about claiming one. He stopped explaining himself. The album sounds like what that decision feels like."
8.9 - a marginal improvement on Love & Hate, more consistent, less filler in the back half, the production pushed further toward cinematic without becoming bombastic. "Hero" is his best opener. "Piano Joint" is his most emotionally precise track. The deduction is for a late-album interlude that earns its existence but little else, and "Final Days" running longer than it needs to. Everything else holds. The Mercury Prize was correct.
Central Thesis
What This Album Is Really About
Identity as Reclamation Soul as Political Form Certainty After Doubt The Long Arc
👑
Identity as Reclamation
Where Love & Hate was about searching for a place to belong, Kiwanuka is about claiming one. He's not asking to be seen; he's telling you what he sees. The title is the whole move: just his name, in full, on the sleeve. Not an explanation or an apology. His name. That's enough. The album is the argument for why.
✊
Soul as Political Form
The Danger Mouse and Inflo production understands that soul music has always been political and stops pretending otherwise. "Hero" opens with strings that could score a film about resistance. "You Ain't the Problem" is the most direct political statement: you are not the problem. The system is the problem. Soul music has been saying this since the 1960s. Kiwanuka says it louder.
🌅
Certainty After Doubt
Love & Hate sounds like someone asking if they're allowed to occupy this much space. Kiwanuka sounds like someone who decided they are. The confidence is earned - three albums in, after the Big Little Lies exposure, after the critical reception - but it's not arrogance. It's the specific confidence of having done the work and stopped waiting for permission.
🏆
The Long Arc
This is his third album. The arc from Home Again to Love & Hate to Kiwanuka traces a man finding his voice, finding his subject matter, and then finding what to do with both. Each record is better than the last. Kiwanuka - just the name - is the arrival. He knew it. Putting his name on the sleeve was him acknowledging it to himself as much as to anyone else.

Rare Frequencies
Things You Didn't Know
01
Mercury Prize Winner
Kiwanuka won the Mercury Prize in 2020. He became the first Black British solo male artist to win it - a fact that says more about the Mercury Prize's history than it does about Kiwanuka's talent. His acceptance speech was brief and without fanfare. He had known for a long time that the album was good. The prize confirmed it for other people.
02
The Title Decision
Kiwanuka has spoken about spending years adjusting himself - his sound, his image, his presentation - to be more palatable to labels, to radio, to imagined audiences. Naming the album just Kiwanuka was the act of stopping that. His surname is from the Buganda clan system in Uganda. Putting it on the sleeve in full was a reclamation of something that had been quietly asked to shrink.
03
The Hero Opening
"Hero" opens the album with a string introduction that reportedly took three full studio sessions to get right. Danger Mouse and Inflo kept pulling it back when it veered toward bombast. The final version lands at the edge of the two - large enough to announce something, restrained enough to leave room for the voice. The voice fills it immediately. The production was waiting for that moment.
Track by Track
The Dissection
01You Ain't the Problem
9.0▼
The opener and the most immediate political statement on the record. He is talking to himself as much as to you. After albums of self-doubt, the directness of this declaration is its own kind of achievement. It opens with groove before it opens with argument. The groove wins you over first. Then the argument lands.
02Rolling
8.8▼
The most immediately gripping track - what certainty feels like when it's pleasurable rather than embattled. The production here is looser than "Hero" or "Piano Joint," more in the pocket. Kiwanuka's voice at its most relaxed. That relaxation is the whole point: he's allowed to take up space without justifying it.
03I've Been Dazed
9.0▼
The album's most groove-driven moment. He's been dazed - by the system, by the noise, by the years of trying to fit - and the song is what it sounds like to come out of that. The production adds texture without cluttering. One of the album's most replayable tracks.
04Piano Joint (This Kind Is Treasure)
9.2▼
The gentlest thing on the record, and the most emotionally precise. The identity argument is made here quietly rather than cinematically - just a piano, his voice, the parenthetical subtitle doing its quiet work. "This kind is treasure." He's referring to a kind of love. He is also referring to himself. The album's most understated track is its most complete.
07Hero
9.4▼
The album's declaration. Strings first - the introduction that took three sessions - then his voice. The word "hero" is not used ironically. He means it in the oldest sense: someone who acts in spite of the cost. The production earns every instrument it deploys. His best opening statement and one of his best tracks.

Footnote
The Mercury Prize went to Kiwanuka in 2020, making him the first Black British solo male artist to win it. The prize was created in 1992. That's a 28-year gap that tells you something about who the prize was for before it was for Michael Kiwanuka. He took it anyway, politely, and continued making music. The album remained correct regardless of the prize. The prize was just catching up.
