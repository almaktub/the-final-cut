Overall Score
"Nine tracks. Every single one iconic. He made a movie soundtrack, a rock album, a pop album, and an R&B album simultaneously - and it's the best version of all four at once."
10.0 because the album refuses to be one thing. Prince was operating at the intersection of every genre he'd absorbed, and Purple Rain is the record where all of those tributaries became one river. Let's Go Crazy is a rock song with a church organ intro and a Hendrix-referencing guitar solo. When Doves Cry is a pop song with no bass. Purple Rain is an eight-minute power ballad that doubles as a stadium anthem and an intimate confession. The Beautiful Ones is a slow-burn R&B track that ends in a primal scream. He held all of that together without a seam. The only argument against 10.0 is that Computer Blue is four and a half minutes of bridge - and even that's better than most artists' greatest moment.

Genre as Demolition
Purple Rain doesn't blend genres - it treats them as raw material. Prince didn't care about genre as identity; he cared about genre as tool. Rock guitar served Darling Nikki. Gospel harmonics served Let's Go Crazy. Synth-pop architecture served When Doves Cry. He picked up each genre's best technology and then set it on fire for his purposes. The result sounds like nothing that came before it because he was making the same move in nine different directions simultaneously.
🕊️
The Doves Cry Decision
When Doves Cry was finished - fully produced, mixed, ready for release - with a bassline. Prince listened to it and removed the bass entirely at the last minute. He said the bass made it sound like everything else. Without it, the track floats. There's nothing underneath the synths and drums, just space. It went to number one and spent five weeks there. Removing the bass was the most important production decision in 1980s pop music.
✝️
Sexuality as Spirituality
Prince understood sex and religion as the same kind of transcendence, and Purple Rain makes that equation explicit. Let's Go Crazy opens with a preacher and ends with a guitar solo. Darling Nikki is about a girl he meets in a hotel lobby and finishes with a backwards gospel message. The album never treats physical desire as separate from spiritual hunger. That synthesis was disruptive in 1984 and it still is.
🎸
The Last Word in Guitar
The guitar solo at the end of Purple Rain is the standard against which all subsequent rock guitar solos are measured. Eric Clapton reportedly called it the best guitar solo he'd ever heard. It was recorded live at First Avenue in Minneapolis, in front of an audience, as part of the film. He played it once. It was the take. The emotion isn't performed - it's what happens when someone plays as if it's the last time.

Track by Track
The Dissection
01 Let's Go Crazy
9.8 ▼
A sermon about joy delivered as a rock song. The "Dearly beloved" opening is not ironic - Prince meant it as a genuine statement of spiritual purpose disguised as a party track. The guitar solo at the end was improvised and kept because it was better than anything planned. The song is four and a half minutes of someone explaining why being alive is worth the trouble, and it makes you believe it every time.
02 Take Me With U
9.6 ▼
The album's quietest moment and its most tender. A duet with Apollonia built around an acoustic guitar figure and a melody so simple it sounds like a memory rather than a composition. Prince understood that the album needed this - something to breathe next to the electricity. It works as a love song and as a palate cleanser. Both functions are necessary.
03 The Beautiful Ones
9.9 ▼
Starts as a restrained synth ballad and ends six minutes later with Prince screaming "Do you want him, or do you want me?" into a void that doesn't answer. The escalation is the structure - the track earns its dissolution. The falsetto at the beginning is pristine; the scream at the end is ragged. Both are the same performance. That range is not common.
04 Computer Blue
9.3 ▼
The longest bridge in pop music history in service of a guitar solo that needed room. The intro dialogue between Prince and Wendy is cryptic enough to feel like it means something and ambiguous enough to mean different things on different listens. The solo that erupts in the final minute is why the track exists: everything before it is runway.
05 Darling Nikki
9.7 ▼
The song that inspired the PMRC and the parental advisory label. Tipper Gore bought Purple Rain for her daughter, heard this track, and started a movement. The backwards message at the end - "Hello, how are you? / I'm fine, 'cause I know that the Lord is coming soon" - reveals the joke and the theology simultaneously. Prince was always both things at once.
06 When Doves Cry
9.9 ▼
No bass. Just drums, synths, and a guitar figure that floats with nothing beneath it. Five weeks at number one. The production decision - removing the bass because the song "sounded too conventional with it" - is the most confident act of artistic self-determination in 1980s pop. The song sounds like nothing before or after. That's the only standard Prince cared about.
07 I Would Die 4 U
9.5 ▼
Two minutes and forty-nine seconds of concentrated energy. The chorus is a declaration, not an explanation. Prince understood that pop worked best when it committed fully to a single feeling and didn't explain it. "I would die for you" is either romantic or religious and the song doesn't choose because he didn't think the distinction mattered.
08 Baby I'm a Star
9.4 ▼
The album's victory lap delivered before the title track. Prince performs it in the film mid-concert, and the joy is not manufactured - the Revolution is locked in, the crowd is there, and what you're hearing is an artist at the exact moment they know the game has changed. The brashness is warranted. It sounds like someone who just won.
09 Purple Rain
9.9 ▼
Recorded live at First Avenue. The guitar solo in the final third was one take, performed in front of an audience, captured as it happened. Eric Clapton heard it and went quiet. The song is a power ballad built like a cathedral - the progression is simple, the arrangement is architectural, and Prince inhabits it with a guitar performance that answers every question the song asks. Eight minutes and forty-one seconds. Every second earns the next.
