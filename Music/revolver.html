The Beatles
Revolver
Parlophone · EMI · 1966

14 tracks. 35 minutes. The Beatles entered the studio in April 1966 as a touring rock band and emerged in June as something that couldn't tour at all. They never played most of these songs live. The album made the live show irrelevant - which turned out to be the most important thing they ever did.

Overall Score
"Revolver is the hinge. Everything before it is one kind of music. Everything after it is another. The album changed what a record was allowed to be - and then became the standard that everything since has been measured against."
10.0 because there is no single album that changed the possibilities of recorded music as completely as this one did in 35 minutes. The studio techniques invented here - ADT, backward guitars, close-miked drums, orchestral arrangements treated as sonic texture rather than decoration - became the grammar of modern recording. Eleanor Rigby and Tomorrow Never Knows sit on the same record. One is a chamber-pop elegy with no guitars. The other is the first electronic dance music record, made in 1966. The fact that they belong together, and that they do belong together, is the whole argument. Sgt. Pepper is more famous. Revolver is better.

The End of Live Music
After Revolver, the Beatles stopped touring permanently. The album made this inevitable - Eleanor Rigby needs a string octet, Tomorrow Never Knows needs a tape loop orchestra, Got to Get You Into My Life needs a brass section. None of it could be replicated live with four people. This wasn't a failure of ambition. It was ambition outrunning the format. They chose the studio and never looked back, and the next four years were the most important in rock history.
💀
Death and Dissolution
Eleanor Rigby is a song about two people nobody notices dying alone. For No One traces a relationship dissolving in real time without dramatic incident - just the slow accumulation of distance. Tomorrow Never Knows, drawn from the Tibetan Book of the Dead, instructs the listener to surrender the ego to the void. This is not the Beatles who wrote She Loves You. Something had shifted in all of them simultaneously, which is the stranger thing.
🎛️
The Studio as Instrument
Geoff Emerick, 20 years old and two weeks into his first job as a full engineer, placed microphones inches from Ringo's drums rather than feet away and changed how drums were recorded forever. ADT - automatic double-tracking - was invented during these sessions to give vocals a processed quality without artificial reverb. Backward guitars appear on three tracks. The studio stopped being a room where performances were captured and became a compositional tool. Revolver is when that happened.
🔺
Three Beatles, Three Arguments
Lennon's songs are about the dissolution of self - Tomorrow Never Knows, She Said She Said, I'm Only Sleeping. McCartney's are about the dissolution of other people - Eleanor Rigby, For No One, Here There and Everywhere. Harrison gets three songs and uses all of them to address systems of control - Taxman (the state), Love You To (eastern philosophy vs western materialism), I Want to Tell You (the failure of language). The album is three solo albums happening simultaneously and they are all correct.

Track by Track
The Dissection
01 Taxman
9.3 ▼
Harrison opens the album - not Lennon, not McCartney - with a guitar riff that Paul McCartney actually played, which is the first joke. The second joke is that the song is about the 95% marginal tax rate the British government imposed on high earners in 1966. Harrison names Harold Wilson and Edward Heath directly. It is the most explicitly political song the Beatles ever released, delivered in a tone of cheerful outrage, and it holds up because the argument is still live.
02 Eleanor Rigby
9.9 ▼
No guitars. No drums. No bass. A double string quartet arranged by George Martin, recorded in a single session. The song describes two people - Eleanor Rigby, who dies alone after a life of invisible service, and Father McKenzie, who buries her and whose sermon nobody hears - and connects them only in death. They never meet in the lyric. They are strangers who are equally invisible. The final question - "where do they all come from?" - is never answered because it is unanswerable. McCartney wrote most of it at 23.
03 I'm Only Sleeping
9.2 ▼
Lennon refused to wake up for sessions before noon, which the other Beatles found infuriating and which gave him time to develop the drowsy, interior quality of this song. The guitar solo was recorded backward - Page took the solo, reversed it, then played it in reverse so when you reverse it back in the track it flows correctly but with a slightly wrong quality. The song sounds like the borderline between sleep and consciousness, which is what it is about. Lennon's most underrated Revolver track.
07 Here, There and Everywhere
9.4 ▼
McCartney wrote it the morning before a session, sitting in Lennon's garden. He was trying to write something in the vein of The Beach Boys' God Only Knows, which he had just heard for the first time. The result is a different kind of perfection than the song he was emulating - less orchestral, more intimate, more plainly emotional. Paul McCartney has called it one of his two or three favorite songs he's ever written. It is a complete love song in two minutes and twenty-six seconds. Nothing is missing.
10 For No One
9.8 ▼
Two minutes and one second. McCartney wrote it about Jane Asher, his girlfriend at the time, in a Swiss ski chalet. It documents the end of a relationship entirely from the outside - no argument, no confrontation, just the clinical accumulation of evidence that it's over. "You stay home, she goes out." The French horn solo was played by Alan Civil, principal horn of the Philharmonia Orchestra, who was asked to play a note above what horn players considered possible. He played it. The impossible note is the album's emotional apex.
11 Got to Get You Into My Life
9.0 ▼
McCartney wrote it as a tribute to marijuana, which is one of the more successful misdirections in pop music. The brass arrangement - six session players from the London soul scene - is the most Stax-influenced thing the Beatles ever recorded. It sounds like a different band entirely. It is a perfectly constructed pop song that happens to be about drugs, which means it is also a perfectly constructed pop song about a new relationship, about religious conversion, about anything that arrives and reorganizes your life. The ambiguity is not accidental.
14 Tomorrow Never Knows
9.9 ▼
The lyrics are drawn almost verbatim from Timothy Leary's psychedelic adaptation of the Tibetan Book of the Dead. The production - tape loops of seagulls, a reversed guitar solo, Ringo's drums processed to sound like a single kick, Lennon's voice through a Leslie speaker cabinet - is the first electronic dance music record. It was made in 1966. It was made in one session. Lennon wanted his voice to sound like "the Dalai Lama singing from a mountaintop." Emerick ran it through a rotating speaker. Correct. The album ends here because there is nowhere further to go.

Footnote
Geoff Emerick was 20 years old when he became the Beatles' recording engineer for Revolver. He had been a full engineer at Abbey Road for approximately two weeks. In the first session, he placed microphones inches from Ringo's drums - violating studio protocol that required mics to be kept at a distance. The studio manager told him to stop. The Beatles told the studio manager to leave. The drum sound Emerick captured in that session became the template for modern rock recording. He had no idea he was inventing anything. He just thought it sounded better.
