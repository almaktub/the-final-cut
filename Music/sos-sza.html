Overall Score
"Sixty minutes of SZA being honest about wanting things she knows she shouldn't want, and refusing to make that honesty tidy."
8.4 because SOS contains at least six songs as good as anything on Ctrl, and is also twenty-three tracks long. The math is the problem. Kill Bill, Shirt, Good Days, I Hate U, Ghost in the Machine, Special - if you cut this to fourteen tracks it's a 9.2. As released it rewards the listener who stays and punishes the listener who can't. The best R&B album of 2022 by a distance, and the most frustrating to rate because the grade is an average of an album that keeps switching between extraordinary and merely decent. SZA is too talented for filler. SOS has some.
Central Thesis
What This Album Is Really About
Desire Without ResolutionObsession as SurvivalThe Ex as MirrorRage Dressed as Pop
🌊
Desire Without Resolution
SOS doesn't give you the part where she moves on. The album sits inside the wanting - the wanting to be loved back, to be right, to be the person she was before things went wrong. No resolution is offered because she doesn't have one. That's the whole record.
🔪
Obsession as Survival
Kill Bill is the thesis statement: I love you so much I might kill you. It's a joke with real edges. The album returns to this territory repeatedly - obsession not as pathology but as the shape of someone who feels things at full volume and can't turn it down.
🪟
The Ex as Mirror
The unnamed other person on most of these songs isn't really the subject - SZA is. The exes and situationships are surfaces she looks at herself in. What she finds is complicated enough that sixty minutes doesn't fully account for it.
🎸
Rage Dressed as Pop
The production is broader than Ctrl - rock guitars, harder drums, genres SZA had no business sounding comfortable in. She sounds comfortable in all of them. The expansion isn't dilution. It's what happens when an artist decides the container was too small.

Track by Track
The Dissection
01SOS
8.5▼
A distress signal as album opener. The production has a rawness the rest of the album sometimes trades for polish. Sets the emotional register correctly: this is someone who needs help and is not entirely sure what kind.
02Kill Bill
9.4▼
The album's signature track for a reason. A love-as-murderous-obsession concept delivered with such lightness that the darkness sneaks up on you. The hook is instant. The implication is not funny at all. The gap between the two is where the song lives.
03Seek & Destroy
8.3▼
The rock energy works here - SZA on a harder production mode she inhabits confidently. Not as sharp as what surrounds it but earns its placement as the momentum track after Kill Bill.
04Low
8.7▼
A vulnerability track that doesn't apologize for the vulnerability. The production creates space around her voice that makes the lyric about being at your lowest feel appropriately exposed.
05Love Language
8.2▼
Works as a mood piece. Not a standout - a connector between the album's stronger runs. The love languages concept is handled with more specificity than the premise usually gets.
06Blind
8.5▼
The production here is some of the album's most interesting - a stripped arrangement that trusts the vocal to carry the emotional weight, which it does.
07Gone Girl
8.4▼
The Gone Girl reference is exact: a woman who has disappeared into someone else's idea of her and is deciding whether to come back. Good track in the middle of an album that has too many good tracks to let any one of them be great.
08Smoking on My Ex Pack
8.0▼
A minor moment. The title is the most interesting thing on the track. Functions as filler in an album that can't afford much of it.
09Nobody Gets Me
8.6▼
One of the more melodically complete tracks on the record. The self-pity in the title is earned by the specificity of the lyric - she's not asking you to agree, just to listen. The distinction matters.
10F2F
8.1▼
Face to face. Physical presence as the only honest form of communication after everything digital has failed. A minor track that makes its point concisely.
11Conceited
8.2▼
Self-love as defense mechanism. The production is the track's best feature - the drums particularly. SZA reclaiming the conceited label with enough swagger that you believe her.
12Special
8.8▼
A quiet devastation. The specific ache of having been exactly what someone needed and still not being chosen. The vocal restraint makes it hit harder than anything louder around it would.
13Notice Me
8.0▼
The most nakedly simple lyric on the album - and it almost works because of it. Sometimes a direct statement of need lands harder than a literary one. This is in the almost column.
14Shirt
9.0▼
A standout late arrival in the tracklist. A sexual confidence track that's also a power reclamation - the shirt as object, as symbol, as thing she doesn't owe back. One of the more original production moments on the album and one of her best vocals.
15Open Arms
8.3▼
The Travis Scott collaboration works better than it should. He doesn't try to match her register and she doesn't condescend to his. A song about second-chance vulnerability with a production that sounds like the 3am it's describing.
16I Hate U
9.1▼
The pre-album single that proved SZA hadn't just replicated Ctrl. The chord progression is the emotional argument - resigned, circular, going somewhere it's been before. One of her most vocally assured performances. The title is the whole thing: she hates him and misses him and those are the same feeling.
17Good Days
8.9▼
The other pre-album single. A psychedelic R&B track about trying to be okay when you're not. The production floats in a way that captures dissociation more accurately than most songs that try. 'I'll wait for good days' is not optimistic - it's patient, which is different.
18Forgiveless
8.4▼
The Ol' Dirty Bastard sample is the best decision on the track. SZA refusing forgiveness as an act of self-preservation rather than pettiness. The energy is harder than most of the album and works for it.
19Too Late
8.1▼
A minor album track. Well executed, not essential. The tiredness of 'too late' as a concept shows at this point in a 23-track record.
20Far
8.5▼
Distance as protection. A late-album track that earns its quietness by arriving when the record has earned the right to go soft. Her vocal here is exceptional - measured in a way that makes the emotion register more precisely.
21Wish You the Worst
8.3▼
Not wishing them well. Not cursing them. Wishing them the worst in that specific register where meanness and honesty become indistinguishable. Gets at a real feeling most break-up songs avoid because it's less sympathetic than the singer.
22Ghost in the Machine
8.6▼
The Phoebe Bridgers collaboration is the album's most unexpected moment and one of its best. Two voices about absence and digital haunting finding a register neither could have built alone. A late-album standout that makes you wish the collaboration had its own EP.
23Used
8.0▼
An understated closer. Not a statement - more like the album getting off the phone while it still can. The quietest ending she could have chosen for sixty minutes of noise. The exhaustion in it is honest.

Footnote
SOS spent ten consecutive weeks at number one on the Billboard 200, the longest run by a female artist in the chart's history at that point. It was preceded by five years of no new studio material. The gap between Ctrl and SOS produced enormous commercial expectation, and SOS met it by refusing to be a safe album. Twenty-three tracks is not a commercial calculation. It's someone who had a lot to say and had been told to wait.
