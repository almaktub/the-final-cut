Overall Score
"JID made a family album about mortality and called it hip-hop. Every technically great rapper has a record like this in them. Not all of them actually make it."
9.4 because The Forever Story is the most emotionally complete album in JID's catalog and one of the best rap records of the decade. He took his family - his brothers, his sister, his neighborhood - and made them mythological without making them symbols. Bruddanem is specifically about his brothers. Sistanem is specifically about his sister. Both work as universal statements because the specificity is so precise. The features are chosen with rare judgment: Yasiin Bey on Stars gives the album its most formally ambitious moment; Lil Wayne on Just in Time is a passing of the torch that actually feels like one. The 0.6 missing: the back half carries slightly less urgency than the front, and a few collaborations don't push JID as hard as the album's best tracks do. But the ceiling here is as high as anything in contemporary rap. JID spent five years on this. Every year shows.

Family as Mythology
JID didn't make a concept album about family. He made an album that treats his actual family as mythology - the brothers, the sister, the neighborhood rendered as specific and undeniable as any myth has ever been. The specificity is what makes it universal. You can only access the general through the particular.
✊
Growing Up Black in Atlanta
The album's geography is precise. These aren't generic hood narratives - they're Southeast Atlanta stories, and the specificity is what makes them resonate outside it. JID doesn't generalize his experience into a statement. He goes deeper into it until it becomes everyone's.
🔁
The Brother Cycle
Bruddanem is the emotional center of the album. JID and his brothers as a repeating pattern - love, loss, loyalty, and the fear that the pattern will break. The track exists in the gap between what you feel for your family and what you're able to do about it. Most songs live outside that gap. This one lives inside it.
💡
Memory as Survival
The Forever Story is about preserving the record of a life before it disappears. JID making the album is itself an act of the album's argument - the story continues as long as someone is telling it. The title is not a metaphor. It is a statement of intent.

Track by Track
The Dissection
01 Galaxy
9.1 ▼
The cosmic opener. JID placing the family story within a larger frame before dropping it to earth. It's a statement of scale - the Forever Story is not a small story. It reaches outward before it goes inward.
02 Raydar
9.3 ▼
JID's technical precision on full display early. The album is telling you what kind of record this is going to be: dense, layered, obsessively crafted. You are going to have to pay attention.
03 Dance Now (feat. Kenny Mason)
9.4 ▼
The kinetic single that announced the album without revealing it. Kenny Mason's presence is the right kind of friction - he pushes JID without pulling the track away from the album's center of gravity.
04 Crack Sandwich
9.6 ▼
The most visceral track. JID at his most technically unhinged - the rhyme schemes are load-bearing architecture in a building that shouldn't stand. It stands. The title is the trap the album is documenting: the economics of survival that eat families from the inside.
05 Can't Punk Me (feat. Earthgang)
9.2 ▼
The Atlanta family reunion. Earthgang and JID are speaking the same language, and the fluency is the point - this is what it sounds like when artists who grew up together and grew up separately meet in the middle of something important.
06 Surround Sound (feat. 21 Savage & Baby Tate)
9.7 ▼
The breakthrough single. 21 Savage does more in one verse than most rappers do in albums. Baby Tate's hook is the correct choice - it opens the track up rather than narrowing it. JID's verse is the best he'd put on a single to that point, which is saying something.
07 Kody Blu 31
9.5 ▼
The first moment the album slows all the way down. Named for JID's brother. The album is arriving at what it actually came to say - and it's taking its time to say it right.
08 Bruddanem (feat. Lil Durk)
9.9 ▼
The album's center. JID's brothers rendered in specific, undeniable detail - not as symbols of a broader point but as actual people with actual weight. Lil Durk's presence is the correct complication: he brings Chicago into an Atlanta story and shows that the geography is different but the grief is the same.
09 Sistanem
9.8 ▼
The sister track. More intimate than Bruddanem and harder to listen to. JID speaking directly to a specific person about specific things - which is always more difficult and more true than speaking generally about love. The production holds space for something that doesn't need to fill space.
10 Can't Make U Change (feat. Ari Lennox)
9.1 ▼
A respite after Sistanem's weight. Ari Lennox's presence softens the track without softening its argument. The album breathing before its final arc. Some of the most emotionally precise tracks need a space like this to land.
11 Stars (feat. Yasiin Bey & BADBADNOTGOOD)
9.9 ▼
Yasiin Bey rapping in 2022 like no time has passed - because when it's this good, time is irrelevant. BADBADNOTGOOD's production is the album's most formally ambitious moment. JID and Bey trading verses is not a passing of the torch - it's two generations recognizing each other across a gap that turns out to be smaller than it looked.
12 Just in Time (feat. Lil Wayne & Kenny Mason)
9.6 ▼
Lil Wayne shows up and proves that the passing-of-torch framing isn't just promotional. His verse is full. Kenny Mason bookends a track that covers three generations of Atlanta and its diaspora in the span of four minutes.
13 Money
9.2 ▼
Late-album clarity. JID on what the whole project has been about - the thing that makes and breaks families, the thing the album has been circling since Crack Sandwich. Named directly. Examined without romance.
14 Better Days (feat. Johnta Austin)
9.3 ▼
The emotional resolution before the close. Johnta Austin's voice carries the hope that JID's verses are too honest to claim outright. The album allowing itself, briefly, to want something better - not because it's earned, but because wanting it is the only thing that makes continuing possible.
15 Lauder Too (feat. Rayvn Lanae)
9.4 ▼
The closing track. The story getting quieter but not stopping. Rayvn Lanae's presence gives the ending a softness that earns its place after everything that preceded it. The Forever Story ends by reminding you that the story was never just JID's. It was everyone in it. It keeps going without him.

Footnote
JID spent five years between DiCaprio 2 and The Forever Story. The gap shows - not in any lost momentum, but in the density of what he had to say. The album is named for the idea that the story doesn't end when the record stops playing. It continues in the people it was made about. JID has been signed to Dreamville since 2017. He used the time.
