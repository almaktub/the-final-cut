0.0
Overall Score
"A man builds a wall around himself and then discovers the wall is the problem. Rock opera as therapy session."
9.4 because The Wall is one of the most ambitious things ever attempted in rock - a double album, a film, a live show with a literal wall built onstage - and it works. The architecture is airtight. The bricks accumulate: dead father, overbearing mother, brutal school, failed marriage. By the time Another Brick completes, you understand exactly how a person closes off. What keeps it from being 10 is that Roger Waters never quite lets the album breathe without him. Comfortably Numb is the one moment where Gilmour's guitar says more than the lyrics. The album would've been different if it let that happen more.
Central Thesis
What This Album Is Really About
Isolation as Architecture Trauma as Building Material Fame as Dissociation The Trial Within
🧱
Isolation as Architecture
The wall isn't a metaphor that gets explained - it's constructed brick by brick across 26 tracks. By the time you arrive at Is There Anybody Out There?, the isolation is physically audible. Waters understood that the only way to make a concept album work is to make the concept structural, not decorative.
🪖
Trauma as Building Material
Roger Waters' father died at Anzio in 1944. Waters was five months old. The album is, at its root, about a grief that was never grievable because it predates memory. Every brick in the wall is something that happened to someone who couldn't process it at the time.
🎭
Fame as Dissociation
Pink the character is a rock star who has come apart onstage. Run Like Hell and Waiting for the Worms show what happens when the wall is finally complete: not peace, but fascism of the self. The album argues that isolation doesn't protect you - it turns you into something worse.
⚖️
The Trial Within
The album climaxes with Pink putting himself on trial. The verdict - tear down the wall - is delivered by his own subconscious. The double album structure exists so that this verdict can carry weight. You've spent 80 minutes building the wall. Tearing it down takes three minutes and is unbearable.

Track by Track
The Dissection
01In the Flesh?
9.2▼
Starts mid-concert. The album opens as if it's already happening - Pink mid-performance, mid-breakdown. The question mark in the title matters: whose flesh, exactly? The reprise at the end of the album answers it, and the answer is chilling.
03Another Brick in the Wall Pt. 1
9.0▼
The first brick: a father who never came home. The acoustic guitar sits alone in the mix. Waters understood that the most devastating thing is restraint. Part 1 is barely two minutes. The wall hasn't started yet - this is the reason it will be built.
06Another Brick in the Wall Pt. 2
9.5▼
The most famous track and still one of the most correct rock songs ever written about institutional cruelty. The children's choir was recorded at Islington Green School. They don't sound oppressed - they sound delighted, which is somehow more disturbing. 'We don't need no education' sung by children in a school. The irony is doing heavy lifting.
13Comfortably Numb
9.9▼
The best song on the album and the one that shows what the album could have been if Waters had trusted Gilmour more. The second guitar solo is one of the greatest thirty seconds in rock. Waters wrote the verses (clinical, numb); Gilmour wrote the choruses (desperate, soaring). The two guitars at the end are a disagreement that resolves into something transcendent.
24The Trial
9.6▼
A show trial conducted by characters from Pink's own life - wife, mother, schoolmaster, all grotesque, all accusatory. Gerald Scarfe's animation in the film turns them into something from a nightmare. The judge is a giant worm. This is not subtle. It is also completely correct.
26Outside the Wall
9.3▼
The album ends with a mandolin and a whispered question: 'Isn't this where we came in?' The last syllable of the record is the same as the first syllable of the record. The wall will be built again. This is the most honest ending Waters could have written.

Footnote
During the final show of the 1977 In the Flesh tour, Waters spat on a fan who was trying to climb the stage. He was so disgusted by his own behavior that he spent the next two years writing The Wall - an album about what fame had done to him. The wall was always self-directed, never at the audience. That's what makes it last.
