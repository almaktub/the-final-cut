Album Autopsy · 054
Foster the People
Torches
Columbia · 2011 · Indie Pop / Synth-Pop
Pumped Up Kicks launched a strange career - the album behind it is uneven but earns its place as a snapshot of a very specific cultural moment
0.0
A debut that overperformed its ambition in the best way: the ratio of memorable songs to filler is better than it has any right to be.
Torches is a document of a very specific early-2010s indie pop moment - the period when the synth was rehabilitated from its 80s excess into something considered tasteful, even cerebral. Mark Foster had been sitting on these songs for years before the project coalesced, and that incubation period shows: the hooks are too solid to be accidental. The problem is that nothing after Pumped Up Kicks quite justifies the fame it generated, and the album's back half loses focus in proportion to how much the front half had. Helena Beat and Houdini would be the centerpieces of most other bands' catalogs. Here they're B-sides to a song that became unavoidable in a way nobody - least of all Foster - planned for. Torches is a 7.9 when it tries and a 9.0 when it doesn't try. The unevenness is honest.
Recurring Themes
🌆
LA as a State of Mind
The album is steeped in a particular version of Los Angeles - not the glamour version, but the sprawl version. The sonic palette (washed-out synths, long reverb tails, bleached-out drums) sounds like driving the 10 freeway at dusk with the windows down. Geography as mood.
😶
Alienation Without Anger
Unlike most alienation records, Torches doesn't sound resentful. The detachment is almost comfortable - which is more disturbing, in context. Pumped Up Kicks describes violence through the frame of a bystander who sounds too calm. That flatness is the point, not a limitation.
🎠
Euphoria with a Catch
The album sounds euphoric. The lyrics frequently aren't. This tension - sad words set to bright music - is the template for a lot of what indie pop became in the decade following Torches. Foster didn't invent the technique, but Torches is one of the cleaner executions of it.
🏄
Youth as Escape Attempt
Don't Stop, Helena Beat, Houdini - the album returns repeatedly to the idea of running, of getting out, of disappearing into something. Where exactly is never specified. The destination doesn't matter. The motion does.
"All the other kids with the pumped up kicks, you'd better run, better run, outrun my gun."
Pumped Up Kicks
Easter Eggs & Annotations
The Perspective
Pumped Up Kicks Was Written from the Shooter's POV
Mark Foster wrote the song from the perspective of a school shooter and set it to an irresistibly catchy melody as a deliberate juxtaposition. The idea was that people would be singing along before they registered what they were singing. He was right. The song became a Top 5 hit before the conversation about its content caught up.
The Buildup
Three Years Between Writing and Release
Torches was largely written between 2008 and 2010, when Foster was writing jingles and working low-level production jobs in LA. The songs existed in demo form for years before the band coalesced around them. The polish on the final record is the result of that extended incubation, not a quick creative burst.
The Moment
The Last Pre-Streaming Indie Pop Breakout
Torches was released at the inflection point between the blog era and the streaming era. Pumped Up Kicks spread via music blogs before it spread via algorithm. Foster the People's career arc would have looked entirely different had the same record been released three years later. Timing matters as much as talent.
The Influence
What Torches Unlocked
The album's production style - bright, airy synths over live drums, vocals buried just slightly in the mix - became a template for a half-decade of indie pop that followed. You can hear Torches in the DNA of a significant percentage of mid-2010s festival pop. Foster has never received credit for this that's proportional to the influence.
Track-by-Track
Pumped Up Kicks
9.4
The hook is so good it obscures the content - which is exactly what it was designed to do. The bassline is among the most recognizable of the decade. The production has aged better than its contemporaries because it was never trying to sound current; it was trying to sound like a particular vibe, and it nailed that vibe definitively. The song will outlast everything else on this record by decades.
Helena Beat
9.0
The album's second-best song and the one that shows the band's range most clearly. The production is denser than anything else here, the vocal melody more elastic. Had this been the lead single, Foster the People would be a different kind of band - more cult, more respected, less ubiquitous. The tradeoff isn't obviously worth it.
Houdini
8.8
The album's most purely kinetic track - built around forward motion, doesn't stop to breathe, doesn't need to. The hook lands on the first listen and lands the same way on the fiftieth. This is craft, not accident. The slight distortion on the vocal in the chorus is the right call and one of the album's better production details.
Don't Stop (Color on the Walls)
8.5
The album's most explicitly joyful track and the one that doesn't try to complicate its own euphoria. Sometimes that's the right call. The parenthetical subtitle - Color on the Walls - is the song's better title and the moment in the lyric it's named after ("I see color on the walls") is the album's most visually specific image. It deserved a better home in the song.
Waste
7.8
Where the album begins to run out of ideas. Not a bad song - the production is clean, the hook serviceable - but the album's back half needed at least one track to surprise, and this isn't it. It sounds like a song written to fill an album rather than one that demanded to be written. The gap between this and Helena Beat is larger than it should be on a debut.

Torches is a record that got famous for the right song but would have been better served by attention to the three songs behind it. Pumped Up Kicks will remain in culture indefinitely - the melody is too strong, the hook too efficient. The album around it is a good debut that the hit single both made possible and, in some sense, overshadowed permanently. Foster the People never quite made the record that justified the career the first single launched. Torches remains the closest they got.
