Avengers: Endgame

dir. Anthony & Joe Russo  ·  2019  ·  Action / Sci-Fi  ·  181 min
8.5
Out of ten
The most satisfying fan-service ever assembled — and also, against all odds, a real movie.

The film earns its ending because it earns its beginning. The first act — grief, failure, five years of aftermath — is the riskiest thing the MCU ever attempted, and it works because the Russos trust the weight of it. Thanos already won. The heroes failed. The world moved on in that broken, half-empty way that loss actually looks like. No studio film at this scale had ever opened on defeat and held there, and the fact that it holds for a full hour is not an accident — it's a structural argument that the emotional payoff at the end will cost something. That cost is what makes the ending land.

The time-travel middle section is deliberately playful, a kind of fan-fiction the film is honest about indulging. The callbacks to earlier films read as nostalgia but they're also doing narrative work — moving pieces into position, giving characters moments of reckoning with their past selves. The final battle delivers what it's supposed to while still managing one genuine surprise: Cap with the hammer, earned not by spectacle alone but by years of quiet setup. Tony's death is the right ending for a character who started as someone who couldn't imagine dying for anything. Robert Downey Jr. underplays it completely, and that restraint is the correct choice.

The film is too long. The time travel logic doesn't survive scrutiny — the rules shift to serve the story rather than the other way around. Some of the fan-service moments are purely mechanical, characters stepping into frame for their moment without drama behind it. None of that kills it. What matters is that the film understands what it's been building toward and has the patience to deliver it with actual weight. Twenty-two films into a franchise, that's not a small thing.

"I am Iron Man."
Tony Stark — Avengers: Endgame
