Final Cut TV No. 02

Breaking Bad

creator Vince Gilligan  ·  2008–2013  ·  Crime Drama  ·  5 Seasons
9.7
Out of ten
A five-season chemistry lesson in how a man convinces himself the villain he's becoming was there all along.

Breaking Bad's trick is patience. Walter White doesn't turn into Heisenberg, he reveals that Heisenberg was underneath the whole time, and Vince Gilligan spends five seasons peeling back one layer of self-justification per episode until there's nothing left but the ego that was driving from episode one. "I did it for my family" stops being true somewhere around season three, and the show never bothers to announce the moment. You just look up one day and the excuse doesn't fit anymore.

The show's other trick is treating consequence like physics. Nothing gets away clean. A stray bullet, a dead fly, a pizza on a roof, every object here eventually pays interest. Jesse Pinkman absorbs the moral weight Walt refuses to carry, and by the finale he's the only character who's actually paid for what he's done rather than rationalized it. Gilligan shoots the New Mexico desert like it already knows how the story ends, all that open sky and nowhere to hide, and the show's best trick is making you root for a man you'd cross the street to avoid in real life.

The half point comes off for a stretch of season two that stalls on plot mechanics (the fly episode is a masterpiece in isolation and a speed bump in sequence), and a finale that ties up almost too neatly for a show built on the idea that actions don't resolve, they compound. Small complaint. Walt's last scene, wandering a meth lab he's no longer welcome in, touching the equipment like it's the only thing he ever loved honestly, is as good an ending as television has produced.

"I am not in danger, Skyler. I am the danger."
Walter White — Breaking Bad
