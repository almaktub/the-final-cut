Final Cut No. 10

Chinatown

dir. Roman Polanski  ·  1974  ·  Crime  ·  130 min
10.0
Out of ten
Film noir resurrected in California sunlight, where the darkness turns out to have been the weather all along.

The detective solves every puzzle, understands everything, and saves no one. That is the whole film, and that is enough to make it the most perfectly constructed crime story ever put on screen. Robert Towne's screenplay gets taught in every film program on earth, and unlike most syllabus monuments, it plays better than it diagrams. Every clue does double duty, every joke is a setup, and the mystery's solution is somehow both a municipal water scandal and the blackest family secret in American cinema. It earns the famous ending because it follows its own logic all the way down, and following it that far required a director willing to be hated for it.

Nicholson is in every scene and wastes not one of them. He plays Gittes as a man who sells himself as a shark and is, quietly, devastated to be proven right about everything. Dunaway plays Evelyn as a woman made entirely of guarded flinches, each one earned. John Huston is in three scenes and creates the most frightening villain of the decade using nothing but courtliness, appetite, and a fish served with its head still on. Jerry Goldsmith wrote the score in ten days after the first one was thrown out and produced the greatest noir music ever recorded: a lonely trumpet over four pianos, romance and rot in the same phrase.

The film is a 10. The man who directed it fled the country three years later rather than face sentencing for a crime against a child. His most chilling speech in this film is about men capable of anything, delivered by one of them. I keep those in separate ledgers because that's the only honest accounting I know how to do. What's on screen: the perfect screenplay, the correct ending, and a final line people quote fifty years later in situations that have nothing to do with movies. That's permanence.

"You see, Mr. Gittes, most people never have to face the fact that at the right time and the right place, they're capable of anything."
Noah Cross — Chinatown
Rewatch verdict
Rewatch It
The first viewing is the mystery. Every one after is the machine: watching Towne lay rails an hour ahead of the train, watching Evelyn's every line mean two things simultaneously.
