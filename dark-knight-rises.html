Final Cut No. 17

The Dark Knight Rises

dir. Christopher Nolan  ·  2012  ·  Action / Drama  ·  164 min
7.8
Out of ten
A trilogy-ender straining under the weight of the film it had to follow.

Bane is interesting in concept. A villain defined by ideology and physical dominance, who breaks the hero at the midpoint and then holds a city hostage while waiting for its residents to choose hope or despair, is a genuinely ambitious construction. Tom Hardy is working hard underneath the mask, you can feel the performance in his shoulders and his stillness, but the audio mix undercuts him consistently. A villain whose menace depends partly on communicating his argument clearly, and who sounds like a man making a speech through a car radio, is a villain the film has partially disarmed itself against. The voice problem was discussed at the time and remains unresolved on any viewing.

Anne Hathaway as Selina Kyle is the film's best thing by a margin. She plays the character as someone who has been surviving for so long that survival has become indistinguishable from personality, and she does it with a lightness the rest of the film rarely allows. The structural choices around Bruce, the injury, the eight years of absence, the pit, work individually as scenes and fail to cohere into a complete arc. The third act is genuinely rousing. The final reveal of Alfred's dream at the cafe is earned. The film has real parts that work.

The problem is context. This was never going to be judged as a standalone action film. It was always going to be the successor to The Dark Knight, and measured against that film it is lesser by a distance that can't be papered over. Where The Dark Knight had a villain who made an argument, this one has a villain whose argument you have to work to hear. Where that film tightened with every scene, this one expands until the connective tissue goes slack. Good work. Wrong film to follow.
