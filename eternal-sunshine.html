Final Cut No. 11

Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind

dir. Michel Gondry  ·  2004  ·  Drama / Sci-Fi  ·  108 min
9.6
Out of ten
A film about how the things we try hardest to erase are the things that made us who we are.

Joel and Clementine aren't exceptional people. That's the whole argument. They fight over small things, they miscommunicate in ordinary ways, and they fall into the specific trap of expecting someone else to complete what's incomplete in yourself. Kaufman's script doesn't dress this up. The relationship we're watching get dismantled, memory by memory, is one we recognize because it's been every relationship that ended before either person was ready, or after one person was past ready and the other hadn't noticed. The non-linear structure isn't a storytelling device for its own sake. It's the thesis made visible: we don't get to choose what stays, and we wouldn't make good choices if we did.

Gondry shoots the erasure sequences with a visual intelligence that trusts the audience. Rooms dissolve behind Joel as he moves through them. Voices bleed from one scene into another. The memories don't collapse cleanly because memory doesn't collapse cleanly, and the film earns that formal choice by keeping the emotion beneath it legible at every moment. Jim Carrey was doing something here that nobody expected from him and that he hasn't quite done since: a performance built entirely from restraint, from the face of a man watching something he loves get smaller. It's one of the better pieces of screen acting from that decade and it gets undersold because the film is so visually alive that the performance gets absorbed into the spectacle.

The film ends at Montauk. They've been told everything, they know what happened and how it ended, and they choose each other anyway. Some readings find this tragic. The film doesn't. It finds it honest, which is harder. The last three words are "okay" repeated twice, and Kaufman earns them completely, because the film has spent its entire running time establishing that okay is not nothing, and that choosing someone with full knowledge of the cost is different from choosing them in ignorance. The beach in the final shot isn't a beginning or an ending. It's the moment before both, and leaving it there is exactly the right decision.

"I could die right now, Clem. I'm just... happy."
Joel Barish — Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind
