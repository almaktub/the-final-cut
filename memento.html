Memento

dir. Christopher Nolan  ·  2000  ·  Thriller / Mystery  ·  113 min
9.4
Out of ten
A film that makes you feel exactly what it's about — and that's the whole point.

The backward structure isn't a gimmick; it's the argument. Nolan puts you in Leonard's position — always arriving at events after the fact, trying to reconstruct cause from effect, uncertain which memories to trust — and the disorientation is the meaning of the film, not a side effect. You know what Leonard knows at any given moment, and no more, and the experience of piecing together what actually happened is identical to his. That's not a formal exercise. It's a commitment to making the audience inhabit the condition rather than observe it from a comfortable distance.

Guy Pearce plays a man who has built a system to replace his lost memory, and the film's central horror is the slow reveal that the system has been compromised by the person running it. Leonard is not a reliable narrator because no one who narrates their own life is. He has chosen what to tattoo on his body, chosen what Polaroids to annotate, and those choices shape what he sees. The black-and-white sequences run forward chronologically; the color sequences run backward. When they meet in the middle you understand something Leonard never will, and you understand it in the same moment he commits to not understanding it.

This is a film about self-deception dressed as a thriller, and it pulls off both simultaneously. Nolan would go on to make bigger films with more resources, but none of them are as precise as this one, as perfectly calibrated between form and content. Every structural choice earns its place. The last image reframes everything before it. You can rewatch it forward and backward and the construction holds either way.

"Memory can change the shape of a room; it can change the color of a car. And memories can be distorted. They're just an interpretation, they're not a record."
Leonard Shelby — Memento
