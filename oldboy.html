Final Cut No. 15

Oldboy

dir. Park Chan-wook  ·  2003  ·  Thriller / Mystery  ·  120 min
9.5
Out of ten
The most formally perfect act of cruelty in cinema history.

The hallway fight is one shot, one corridor, one man who can barely stand, and it says everything the film has to say about will versus futility. Oh Dae-su doesn't win through strength or skill. He wins because he won't stop, and there's no glory in it. Park Chan-wook shoots it with a lateral camera that tracks the violence like a bored observer, which is exactly the right choice: the fight is not exciting in the way action fights are exciting. It's exhausting in the way that survival is exhausting. By the time it ends you feel it in the same way Dae-su does, which is the point.

The visual grammar throughout is meticulous. Every frame is composed with the precision of someone who knows where the story is going and is arranging the evidence in advance. The film is about the violence of obsession, and specifically about the way revenge devours the person pursuing it before it gets anywhere near its target. Woo-jin Lee is not a simple villain. He is a man who has organized fifteen years of his life around a single act of destruction, and when you understand what was taken from him, the act becomes explicable without becoming forgivable. That distinction is where the film lives.

The twist is not the film's achievement. The achievement is that the twist feels inevitable rather than cheap, because the film has been building toward it with every image. You don't watch Oldboy and feel good afterward. You feel like you've been given access to something about human nature, specifically about what people are capable of when pain becomes the organizing principle of a life, that you'd have been comfortable not knowing. Park knew that. He made the film anyway.

"Laugh and the world laughs with you. Weep and you weep alone."
Oh Dae-su — Oldboy
