Final Cut No. 2

Pulp Fiction

dir. Quentin Tarantino  ·  1994  ·  Crime  ·  154 min
9.3
Out of ten
A hangout movie wearing a crime movie's jacket, shuffled so that a redemption gets the last word.

Thirty years of imitators and not one of them figured out the trick. It's not the structure, which everyone copied. It's the temperature: Tarantino lets scenes run eight minutes on pure conversation, and the conversation is so alive you don't notice you're being set up. Hitmen discuss foot massages with the seriousness of theologians, and then the theology shows up for real. Every digression turns out to be the plot. People have got lines from this tattooed on themselves, which is its own review.

Samuel L. Jackson gives one of the great American performances: a sermon machine who slowly realizes, mid-speech, that he means it. Travolta plays Vincent half-asleep on purpose and made it a career resurrection. Uma Thurman built an icon out of a bob and a cigarette. The film has no original score, just crates of surf rock and soul dropped with sniper precision. Misirlou as an opening gun. "Girl, You'll Be a Woman Soon" scoring an overdose. It rewired how movies use pop music, for better and in lesser hands much worse.

It's not the best film of the 90s, but it rearranged the furniture. The 9.3 rather than higher: the pawn shop basement trades the film's wit for pure transgression, and Tarantino casting himself to deliver his ugliest dialogue has aged badly. The film survives it. Where it soars is every minute Jackson is on screen, the diner standoff above everything, a man reciting scripture for the second time but now actually thinking about the words, talking two robbers out of dying and himself into a new life in the same breath. The chronology means the rewatch is a different film: you know Vincent's future during every scene he's alive, and the ending gets heavier each time.

"The truth is, you're the weak, and I'm the tyranny of evil men. But I'm tryin', Ringo. I'm tryin' real hard to be the shepherd."
Jules Winnfield — Pulp Fiction
