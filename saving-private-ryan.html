Final Cut No. 46

Saving Private Ryan

dir. Steven Spielberg  ·  1998  ·  War / Drama  ·  169 min
9.5
Out of ten
The first 27 minutes alone justify any discussion of the greatest films ever made.

The D-Day opening sequence is the most accurate and harrowing combat sequence in cinema history, and Spielberg shoots it with a desaturated, kinetic style that puts you inside the chaos rather than above it. There is no safe observational distance. The camera is at soldier height, shaking with the same terror, framing not the battle but fragments of it: a floating arm, a man looking for his own, the water pink with blood. Janusz Kaminski's photography stripped the color and the romanticism simultaneously, and the result is 27 minutes of war that function less as cinema than as physical experience. You don't watch it; it happens to you.

The film becomes a different register after that, quieter and more conventional, a road-movie structure through occupied France. That transition has been criticized as a let-down and the criticism isn't entirely wrong, but it misses what Spielberg is doing: he's giving you room to breathe so the violence means something when it returns. Tom Hanks' Captain Miller is one of the great screen soldiers, competent and human, keeping something private in him that only one person ever gets to hear. The men around him are specific and alive in the way that ensemble war films rarely achieve. You know enough about each of them to feel what the film intends you to feel when it counts.

The final battle in Ramelle is visceral and earned, a different kind of chaos than Omaha because now you know the people inside it. The question the film ends on, "was I a good man?", is the only question war films that are honest about their subject should be asking. Not whether the war was right, not whether the mission was worth it in strategic terms, but whether the individual, in the accumulation of impossible choices, managed to remain human. The old man at the grave, uncertain whether he deserves the sacrifice made for him, is the image the film has been building toward the whole time.

"Earn this."
Captain Miller — Saving Private Ryan
