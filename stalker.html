Final Cut No. 9

Stalker

dir. Andrei Tarkovsky  ·  1979  ·  Drama  ·  162 min
10.0
Out of ten
A film about faith made by a man who understood that doubt is what faith is made of.

The Room grants your deepest desire. Not your announced desire — your actual one. This distinction destroys a man in the film's backstory, a porcupine who entered asking for his dead brother's return and received a fortune instead, revealing what he really wanted. No one in Stalker's present journey reaches the Room. They turn back before the threshold and the film lets this be enough, which is an act of profound artistic restraint and also the only honest ending available. You cannot show a room that reveals the truth about a person without lying about what truth looks like.

Tarkovsky films the Zone in color and everything outside it in sepia. The Zone is not hostile; it's alive. It rearranges itself according to the hopes and fears of whoever moves through it, so the path that worked yesterday will kill you today. The Stalker navigates by faith, throwing a bolt ahead to test ground that has no stable ground. He has been there many times and never entered the Room. He leads others to it and cannot go himself. Whether this is humility or cowardice is the film's central unresolved question. The Writer and the Professor represent different corruptions of intellect: one nihilism, one the arrogance of the technician. The Stalker believes. The Zone doesn't reward him for it. It doesn't punish him either. The Zone doesn't care.

The film's final image — the Stalker's daughter moving glasses across a table without touching them, Beethoven's Ode to Joy rising on the soundtrack — lands differently depending on what you've brought into the theater. It's either miraculous or haunting or both. Tarkovsky provides no guardrails. He made the film while dying from the same industrial chemicals used on location and finished it knowing it. That fact doesn't belong in a review and I can't leave it out.

"Let them believe. And let them laugh at their passions. Because what they call passion is not really the energy of the soul, but merely friction between the soul and the outside world."
Stalker — Stalker
